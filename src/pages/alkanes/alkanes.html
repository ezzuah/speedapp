ALKANES

You have once had or perhaps seen someone been given a drink in a plastic cup. Do you have a dress made of nylon, polyester or rayon? You may have ridden to school in a car that probably has synthetic rubber tires. Even the body of your ballpoint pen is plastic.


These are things that are common to us in our everyday routines but interestingly, these materials―synthetic fibers, synthetic rubber, plastics―are produced from petroleum. Petroleum is almost entirely composed of hydrocarbons. The study of organic chemistry is best started by examining these compounds. Organic Chemistry is the study of organic compounds. It is concerned with matter obtained from natural or living sources.


Organic compounds are compounds containing carbon. Examples of organic compounds are ethane (C2H6), propane (C3H6), propyne (C3H4), etc. Note that other compounds such as cyanide (CN-), carbon (II) oxide (CO), carbon (IV) oxide (CO2) and carbonates (CO32-) have carbon but are not organic compounds.

Hydrocarbons

These are organic compounds that contain carbon and hydrogen only. Hydrocarbons are classified into families of compounds based on their functional groups. Functional group is an atom or group of bonded atoms which give an organic compound its characteristics chemical properties. Hydrocarbons are grouped as aliphatic (without ordour) or aromatic (those with ordour) hydrocarbons. In this lesson we will consider only aliphatic hydrocarbons. These are;

  1. Alkanes: they contain only single bonds

  2. Alkenes: they contain at least a single carbon–to–carbon double bond.

  3. Alkynes: they contain at least a single carbon–to–carbon triple bond.


These three hydrocarbons are known as aliphatic hydrocarbon, because they have no scent(smell).


Alkanes (CnH2n+2)

Alkanes also known as paraffins are hydrocarbon having only single bonds through out their molecule. They are said to be saturated; that is, each carbon atom is bonded to the maximum number of hydrogen atoms with no double or triple bonds between the carbon atoms.


The first four members of the alkane series are methane (CH4), ethane (C2H6), propane (C3H8) and butane (C4H10). After butane, the members are named using the Latin prefix for the number of carbon atoms present in the molecule and the ending of the family name is –ane. Pentane contains five carbon atoms; hexane, six carbon atoms; heptane, seven carbon atoms and so on.














Note: Each carbon atom is singly bonded to another carbon or hydrogen atom.


Any hydrocarbon (compound) with C—C single bond throughout is said to be a saturated hydrocarbon and saturated hydrocarbons are very unreactive. Alkanes, are very unreactive due to their saturated nature and therefore do not undergo addition reaction but undergo substitution reaction. This means that, we cannot add any more atoms, say Br or Cl to an alkane unless the H bonding to the C is taken off and replaced or substituted with another atom.

Substituents

If a halogen (Fluorine, Chlorine, Bromine or Iodine) occurs as substituent, they are named as follows;

  1. Fluorine, F is called Fluoro

  2. Chlorine, Cl is called Chloro

  3. Bromine, Br is called Bromo

  4. Iodine, I is called Iodo



If an alkyl group occurs as substituent, they are named as follows

  • CH3 – methyl

  • CH2CH3 – ethyl

  • CH2CH2CH3 – propyl and so on.



Note: An alkyl group lacks one of the hydrogen atoms of an alkane. Methyl CH3–) is an alkyl group derived from methane (CH4).


Naming Alkanes with IUPAC Rules



  1. Pick out the longest continuous carbon chain and find the IUPAC name. It is hexane and heptane for examples a) and b) respectively. This is the parent alkane from which the whole IUPAC name is to be derived.

  2. Identify the substituent(s) attached to the parent chain. In the examples, the hexane chain bears a methyl (CH3) group whereas the heptane bears two bromine atoms and an ethyl group.

  3. Number the carbon atoms (in the longest continuous chain) so as the atom bearing the first substituent will have the lowest number. Consider example a), if the pattern of the red numbers is followed, the atom bearing the methyl group will have a number of four. Whereas that of the black numbering will give that same atom a number of 3. This rule will expect us to number in the black pattern because that gives the lowest number to the atom bearing the first substituent. Therefore the name of the first compound is 3–methylhexane.

    This rule must be applies to the second compound. However it is not enough to name it, so we apply the fourth and fifth rules as well.

  1. If the same substituents occur more than once, indicate this by using the prefix di, tri, tetra etc. to show how many of the substituents are present and indicate the positions of each substituent with numbers 2, 3, 4, etc. Applying this rule to the bromo atoms gives: ‘2, 5–dibromo…’ because the positions of the bromine atoms are on the second and the fifth carbon atoms.

  2. If there are several different groups attached to the parent chain, name them in alphabetical order. In this case, we have an ethyl group on the fourth carbon (4–ethyl) aside the bromine atoms. Adding this rule gives the complete name of the second compound as: 2, 5–dibromo–4–ethylheptane.


Isomers and Isomerism

Compounds that have the same molecular formulae or composition but different structural formulae are called Isomers. Isomerism is the occurrence of two or more compounds with the same molecular formula but different structural formulae. Butane and methylpropane are isomers because they all have a molecular composition of four Carbon and ten hydrogen atoms. But each one has its own unique properties and structural formula.


The number of isomers a compound can have increases greatly as the number of carbon atoms increases.

Homologous Series

This refers to a class of compounds which have the same general formula and exhibit similar chemical properties. Examples are the alkane, alkene and alkyne compounds. All the other mentioned functional groups form a homologous series.


Characteristics or Properties of homologous series

  • All members of the group have the same general formula. For example, the alkane series is represented by the formula CnH2n + 2, Alkenes by CnH2n and Alkynes, CnH2n – 2.

  • All members have the same functional group

  • Each member differs in molecular formula from the next by CH2

  • All members have similar chemical properties or react in a similar way but they differ in physical properties. For example, as the molecular mass of the compounds in a series increases, the boiling point increases as well.

  • All members have similar method of preparation.
