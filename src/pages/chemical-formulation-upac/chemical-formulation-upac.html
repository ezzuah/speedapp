Chemical Formula and IUPAC Naming

We learnt in the first chapter that, each element has a short hand form of representation known as its chemical symbol. Do you have an idea about the short hand form for compounds? Is there any? Yes there is.

There short hand form for compounds is chemical formula. A chemical formula is a concise way of expressing information about the atoms that constitute a particular chemical compound.

The formula shows two things; The elements presents in a compound and the relative number of atoms of each element in the compound.

For example, experiments have revealed that two atoms of hydrogen will combine with one atom of oxygen to form a single water molecule.

Thus, the formula for water is written as H2O. The subscript '2' after the 'H' indicates that there are two hydrogen atoms in one molecule of water.


Note that there is no subscript after the Oxygen (O). If a symbol has no subscript, it is understood that only one atom of that element is present in the molecule.

As chemical symbols represent elements, so do chemical formulas represent compounds.

Atoms have certain number of electrons they will gain, loose or share when forming molecules or ions. This number is the oxidation number of the atom. Usually, oxidation number is defined for an ion as the charge on it. It can be determined by applying the octet rule.


In the pictures below, if the beryllium wants to have a stable structure, it will be easier for it to loose its two valence electrons than to accept six electrons.

Beryllium will then loose its two outer electrons to have the K shell fully filled. After loosing two electrons;

The proton number = 4 = +4
The electron number = 2 = -2
The effective charge = +2.

Beryllium will then have a charge of +2. So the oxidation number of beryllium is +2.


In a similar vane, the fluorine will accept one electron to have its L shell fully filled and will have oxidation state of -1. After fluorine looses one electron;

the number of protons = 9
the number of electrons = 8
The effective charge = -1..

Now, if beryllium is to bond (combine) with fluorine, a single atom of beryllium will need to donate two electrons to two different fluorine atoms. The chemical formula for beryllium fluoride will then be written as BeF2 .

Note that the whole BeF2 molecule is neutral because the +2 charge of beryllium is cancelled by the -1 charge of the two fluorine atoms.


Writing Chemical Formulas

Binary compounds (two atoms). Example, calcium chloride

1. Determine the valences (oxidation numbers) of the given elements (ignore charges).

2. Write the valences of elements against the chemical symbol as in stage 1 above.

3. Write the valence of the cation as a subscript for the anion and vice-versa.

Note that the subscript is ignored if it is '1', as in the case of Ca.


A cation and a polyatomic anion; example, Aluminum Sulphate

The steps in the first example are repeated. However, the anion goes into a bracket.

The ‘2’ and ‘3’ indicates that there are two aluminum atoms and three units of the sulphate.

This also means that for a complete reaction between aluminum and sulphate, two parts of aluminum will react with three parts of sulphate.


NAMING OF COMPOUNDS

When naming binary (two elements) ionic compounds, the cation is named first and then the anion.

When an elemental anion is named, the suffix, -ide, is added to the name of the element. For instance, chlorine becomes chloride; sulphur, sulphide and oxygen, oxide.


There are two common types of cations: Type I and Type II. Type I cations have only one oxidation state and their name is simply listed when the compound is named. Type II cations have more than one oxidation state. When the ionic compound is being named, the cation is named first and a Roman numeral written in parenthesis is used to denote the charge (oxidation state) of the cation, followed by the name of the anion.


Example, Fe2O3 is named as iron (III) oxide. The steps below can be used to calculate the oxidation state of the iron (Fe).

Step 1: Represent the charge of the iron with a variable, let’s say x; Fe = x

Step 2: estimate the total charge of the oxygen atoms; 3 (-2) = -6

Step 3: sum up the variable and the total charges on the oxygen and equate to the total charge on the ionic unit (Fe2O3); 2 (x) + (-6) = 0

Step 4: solve for x; x = 6/2 = 3


Oxidation states calculation for oxoanions and naming.

Oxoanions are anions which contains oxygen atoms that are usually bonded to a central atom.

Example is the SO42- ion. The oxidation state of the central atom, which is sulphur (S), can be calculated as follows.

Step 1: Represent the charge of the central atom with a variable, let’s say x; S = x

Step 2: estimate the total charge of the oxygen atoms; 4 (-2) = -8

Step 3: sum up the variable and the total charges on the oxygen and equate to the total charge on the ion: x + (-8) = -2

Step 4: solve for x; x = -2 – (-8) =6

Naming rules for oxoanions (using the SO42- as an example)

1. The suffix ‘ate’ replaces the last two or three letters of the central atom; sulphur will change to sulphate.

2. The oxidation state of the central atom is written in roman numerals and enclosed in parenthesis; (VI)

3. The prefixes monooxo, dioxo, trioxo or tetraoxo is used to represent the number of oxygen atoms present in the anion.

In this case we have four oxygen atoms therefore Tetraoxo will be used. The name of the anion is Tetraoxosulphate (VI) ion.
