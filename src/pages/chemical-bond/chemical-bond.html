CHEMICAL BOND


In the previous topic, we discussed the atomic structure, and closed the chapter with electron configuration. It was clear at the end (of the chapter) that the duplet (two valence electrons) and octet (eight valence electrons) structures are the stable electronic configurations.


All atoms want to have that form of stability, with the exception of helium (which already has duplet), and neon, argon, krypton, xenon and radon (which also have octets). Since the named atoms have stable electronic structures they are unreactive (that is, they hardly go into bonding).


Atoms (without stable structures) attain stability through interatomic bonding only. Interatomic bonds are forces that hold two or more atoms together to form a single particle. The three types of interatomic bonds are covalent, ionic and metallic bonds.


Ionic or Electrovalent Bond

Some atoms (of elements) have strong affinity (attraction) for electrons and are called electronegative atoms. Others also have very little affinity for electrons and they are called electropositive atoms. When an electronegative atom bonds with an electropositive atom, an electron(s) is pulled out of the electropositive atom by the electronegative atom. The loss of electron leaves a positive charge(s) on the electropositive atom and the electronegative attains a negative charge.


Note: When an atom loose or gain electron, it becomes an ion. A positively charged ion is called a cation and a negatively charged ion is an anion.


These charges (positive and negative) then attract each other. The attraction constitutes the ionic bond. Ionic bond is an electrostatic force of attraction between a cation and an anion. Note that the number of valence electrons in both sodium and chlorine after the electron transfer is eight (octet structure). Ionic bond exists in compounds only and compound whose building blocks are ionic unit are called ionic compounds. Example is sodium chloride, magnesium oxide, etc.




Covalent bond

This is a bond formed as a result of sharing of valence electrons between two atoms. The electronegativity difference between the bonding atoms must be small or negligible. Particles held together by covalent bonds are called molecules. Covalent bonds exist in both elements (hydrogen, nitrogen, bromine, etc.) and compounds (water, glucose, candle wax, etc.). Compounds that have molecules as their building blocks are called molecular compounds. Examples are the water and hydrogen molecules as shown below. Hydrogen molecules will aggregate to form hydrogen element and water molecules will form water (as a compound).

Metallic bond

Electrons are kept in their orbitals as a result of attraction between them and protons in the nucleus of an atom. Most metals have 1, 2 or 3 valence electrons and they easily loose them because of the weak attraction of the protons on these electrons. This weak attraction of metals on their valence electrons helps in the formation of metallic bonds. Since the valence electrons are weakly held in the metal atom, they fall out of the atom into spaces within the metallic crystal.


The valence electrons are then said to be delocalised, because they are not held in one locality or as part of a specific ion or bond. Delocalization transforms the metal atoms into cations. A force of attraction is then built between the delocalised electrons and the cations. This force of attraction constitutes the metallic bond. Metallic bond is therefore defined as the attractive force between the positively charged ions and the delocalized electrons of a metal. The free movement of free or delocalized electrons makes metals good conductors of heat and electricity.

Molecular Mass and Formula Mass

Atoms have masses and it is known as atomic mass. When atoms combine to form a molecule, the molecule will have a mass which will be equal to the sum of the masses of the individual atoms making up the molecule. Such a mass is called the molecular mass. This name is incorrect when applied to an ionic substance. Sodium chloride, NaCl, is an ionic substance and does not exist in molecular form. A better name for the mass of an ionic substance is formula mass. For example, the atomic mass of one hydrogen atom is 1u and that of oxygen is 16u. Chemical bond is a force that holds two or more atoms together as a result of a shift or transfer of valence electrons between the atoms. There are two types of chemical bonds. They are interatomic (between atoms or hold atoms) and intermolecular (between molecules or hold molecules) bonds.


INTERMOLECULAR BONDS

After the various building blocks (that is, ions or molecules) of matter have been formed, they are held together by other forces. In ionic compounds, the ionic bonds exist throughout the entire compound; as a result, all ionic compounds are solids at room temperature. This is not so for molecular compounds. After the molecules have been formed through interatomic bonding, another force (intermolecular bonds) holds the molecules together to form the entire material. Intermolecular bonds are forces that hold molecules together. The strength of intermolecular bonds gives rise to three different physical properties known as the states of matter. The three states of matter are solids, liquids and gases.


Solids are objects that have definite volume and shape as a result of strong intermolecular bonds holding the building blocks together. The particles of a solid vibrate at their fixed positions and this is the only movement they undergo. Examples of solid are wood, stone, cup, etc.


Liquids are substances which have definite volume but no definite shape. The particles are loosely packed due to relatively weak intermolecular forces. Unlike solids, the particles of a liquid can slide over each other and this makes liquids flow. Examples are water, petrol, kerosene, etc.


Gases are substances with no definite volume or shape. Their building blocks are far apart. The intermolecular forces holding particles of a gas together are negligible. Examples are air, chlorine gas, water vapour, etc.


Any substance can exist in any of the three states of matter depending on the physical conditions (temprature and pressure) it is subjecred to. As the temperature of a substance increases, the strength of the intermiolecular forces holding its particles together decreases. For instance, water at a temperature of 0oC and a pressure of 1 atmosphere exist as solid(ice, strong intermolecular forces), at 25oC and a pressure of 1 atmosphre, it exist as a liquid(liquid water, relatively loose intermolecular forces)and at 100oC and 1 atmosphere pressure it exist as a gas(water vapour, strength of intermolecular forces is neglegible).
