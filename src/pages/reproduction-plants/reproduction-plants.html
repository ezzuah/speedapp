REPRODUCTION AND GROWTH IN PLANTS


We’ve been seeing our mangoes, oranges, plantains, etc still in existence and of the same characteristics just like we first met them. This means that they also reproduce and have offsprings. But how? Consider first, the reproductive organ of a plant—the flower.



Some flowers (like the one here) are bisexual—having the female and male parts on the same flower. Unisexual flowers are those that have only one of the sexes, either female or male.


Petals are what we mostly see at first glance of a flower. They are the brightly coloured part of a flower. Petals are used to attract insects into the flower; they may also be scented. More than one petal is called corolla.


Sepals are leaf–like structures that surround and protect the flower before it blooms. More than one sepal are called calyx.


The receptacle is the part of the branch on which a flower forms or the flower's attachment to the stalk. In some cases the receptacle becomes part of the fruit after fertilisation. The stigma, style, ovary, and ovule are often known collectively as the carpel or female part of the flower (also the gynoecium).


The top of the stigma is often sticky and is where the pollen grains attaches. The style is the long tube that attaches the stigma to the ovary. The style raises the stigma away from the ovary to decrease the likelihood of pollen contamination.


It varies in length. Sperm from the pollen will travel down this tube to the ovules. The ovule is like the egg in animals and once fertilization has taken place will become the seed. The ovules are stored in the ovary until they are fertilized.


Plants can only fertilize eggs of the same species. Special chemicals prevent sperm from fertilizing the eggs of flowers that are not the same kind. The ovary protects the ovule and once fertilisation has taken place it will become the fruit.


The stamen, which consists of anther and filament, is the male part of the flower (also Androecium). An anther produces pollen grains and a filament which is the stalk of the Anther supports it. Pollen grains produced by the anther are carried by insects, wind or other animals to the carpel where it may fertilize the eggs.


Most flowers hold some sugary solution called nectar which attracts insects (see picture). A flower stack gives support to the flower and elevates the flower for insects to see. A floral formula is a way to represent the structure of a flower using specific letters, numbers, and symbols.



The following representations are used: Ca = calyx (e.g. Ca5 = 5 sepals), Co = corolla (e.g. Co6 = 6 petals), A = androecium (stamens; e.g., A2 = 2 stamens), G = gynoecium (carpel or carpels; e.g., G1 = 1 carpel/ monocarpous). A floral formula may look like this: Ca5 Co6 A2 G1.Note that several additional symbols are sometimes used. For example x: to represent a "variable number", ∞: to represent "many".


Pollination is an important step in the reproduction of plants: the transfer of pollen from the anther to the female stigma. The anthers contain pollen sacs. The sacs release pollen on to the outside of the anthers that brush mostly against insects on entering the flowers.


When deposited pollen is transferred to the stigma of another flower, then cross pollination has occurred. But when pollen is transferred onto the stigma of the same flower, it is called self pollination. Biotic pollination, occurs when pollination is mediated by an organism, called a pollinator.



Entomophily, pollination by insects, often occurs on plants that have developed bright petals and a strong scent to attract insects such as, bees, wasps, butterflies, etc. In Zoophily, pollination is done by vertebrates such as birds and bats.


Plants adapted to this strategy tend to develop red petals to attract birds and rarely develop a scent because few birds have a sense of smell. Abiotic pollination occurs when pollination is mediated without the involvement of other organisms. Only 10% of flowering plants are able to pollinate without animal assistance.


For example, anemophily is pollination by wind. This form of pollination is very common in grasses and many deciduous trees. Hydrophily is pollination by water and occurs in aquatic plants which release their seeds directly into the surrounding water.


About 80% of all plant pollination is biotic. Of the 20% of abiotically pollinated species, 98% is by wind and 2% by water. It is after pollination that the ovule is able to be fertilized.


Fertilization

After the pistil is pollinated, the pollen grain germinates. From each pollen grain, a pollen tube grows out attempting to travel into the ovary by creating a path through the female tissue (stigma and style).


Enzymes are secreted by the pollen tube to digest the female tissue as the pollen tube grows. The pollen tube does not directly reach the ovary in a straight line. It travels near the skin of the style and curls to the bottom of the ovary.


Near the receptacle, the pollen tube breaks through the micropyle (an opening in the ovule wall) and reaches the ovum (or egg cell) to fertilize it. This is the point when fertilisation actually occurs. After being fertilized, the ovary starts to swell and will develop into a fruit while the ovules becomes the seeds. Note that pollination and fertilisation are two separate processes.


Simple fruits (either dry or fleshy) results from the ripening of a simple or compound ovary with only one pistil. Dry fruits may be either dehiscent (opening to discharge seeds), or indehiscent (not opening to discharge seeds).


Examples of simple dry fruits are: wheat, pea, bean, carrot, etc. Fruits in which part or all of the pericarp (fruit wall) is fleshy at maturity are simple fleshy fruits. Examples of simple fleshy fruits are: tomato, avocado, banana, apple, pear, etc.


An aggregate fruit develops from a flower with numerous simple pistils. An example is the strawberry, blueberry, raspberry, etc. A multiple fruit is one formed from a cluster of flowers (called an inflorescence). Each flower produces a fruit, but these mature into a single mass. Example is the pineapple.

Seed Dispersal

Flowering plants reproduce themselves by producing seeds. The seeds also provide the plants with a way to spread out and grow in new places, sometimes a long way from the parent. This is important because if the seeds are not dispersed, many germinating seedlings will grow very close to the parent plant.


This results in competition between every one of the seedlings as well as with the parent plant. The competition is for light, space, water and nutrients and all of these are important for plants to be able to grow. Seeds can be dispersed in a number of different ways. They may be carried by wind, water or animals.


Some plants even shoot the seeds out explosively. In fact, seed size is an important factor. Some fruits have coats covered with spikes either to prevent themselves from being eaten by animals or to stick to the hairs, feathers or legs of animals, using them as dispersal agents.



The sweet flesh of many fruits is appealing to animals, so that the seeds held within are eaten and carried away and deposited at a distance from the parent.


Likewise, the nutritious, oily kernels of nuts are appealing to rodents (such as squirrels) who hoard them in the soil in order to avoid starving, thus giving those seeds that remain uneaten the chance to germinate and grow into a new plant away from their parent.


Other fruits are elongated and flattened and so become thin, with wings or tiny parachutes. This is a mechanism to increase dispersal distance away from the parent via wind. Some other fruits can disperse via water.


Fruits like coconut can float thousands of miles in the ocean to spread seeds. Other fruits also do this by explosively casting the seeds out. In all, the seeds of flowering plants vary in size. Some are as small as grains of salt while others may be almost the size of tennis.


The difference in size reflects differences in the amount of food reserves stored in the seed for the benefit of the embryo plant inside. Usually, the larger the seed, the more food reserves it contains. This allows the germinating seed and young seedling more time to grow.


It can then become well established before it must begin manufacturing its own food. The longer a seedling has before it must become self sufficient, the greater chance it has of becoming successfully established.


A seed is a small embryonic plant enclosed in a covering called the seed coat, usually with some stored food. A typical seed includes three basic parts: an embryo, a supply of nutrients for the embryo, and a seed coat.


The embryo is an immature plant from which a new plant will grow under proper conditions. The embryo has one or two cotyledons or seed leaves. The radicle is the embryonic root. The plumule is the embryonic shoot.


The embryonic stem above the point of attachment of the cotyledon(s) is the epicotyl. The embryonic stem below the point of attachment is the hypocotyl. Within the seed, there usually is a store of nutrients for the seedling that will grow from the embryo.



The form of the stored nutrition varies depending on the kind of plant. In most plants, the stored food is called the endosperm. In some species, the embryo is embedded in the endosperm which the seedling will use upon germination. The seed coat (or testa) develops from the tissue originally surrounding the ovule.


The seed coat in the mature seed can be a paper–thin layer or something more substantial. The seed coat helps protect the embryo from mechanical injury and from drying out. In addition to the three basic seed parts, some seeds have an appendage (external body part that projects from the body) on the seed coat.


It can be an aril (as in nutmeg), hairs (as in cotton), etc. There may also be a scar on the seed coat, called the hilum; it is where the seed was attached to the ovary wall.


Seed Germination

This is the process of growth of the embryo into a functional plant. It involves the reactivation of the metabolic pathways that lead to growth and the emergence of the radicle or seed root and plumule or shoot.


Three fundamental conditions must exist before germination can occur.


  1. The embryo must be alive (called seed viability).


  2. Any dormancy of other seeds that prevent germination must be over come.


  3. The proper environmental conditions must exist for germination.


Water, oxygen, temperature, light, etc are all environmental conditions necessary for germination. The part of the plant that emerges from the seed first is the embryonic root, termed radicle or primary root.


This allows the seedling to become anchored in the ground and start absorbing water. After the root absorbs water, the embryonic shoot emerges from the seed. The shoot comprises three main parts: the cotyledons (seed leaves), the section of shoot below the cotyledons (hypocotyl), and the section of shoot above the cotyledons (epicotyl).


The way the shoot emerges differs between plant groups. In epigeous (or epigeal) germination, the hypocotyl elongates and forms a hook, pulling rather than pushing the cotyledons through the soil. Once it reaches the surface, it straightens and pulls the cotyledons and shoot tip of the growing seedlings into the air.


Beans are an example of plants that germinate this way. Another way of germination is hypogeous (or hypogeal) where the epicotyl elongates and forms the hook. In this type of germination, the cotyledons stay underground where they eventually decompose. Peas, for example, germinate this way.


The structure of flowering plants differs according to whether they are monocots or dicots. The term monocot and dicot refers to the number of cotyledons there are in a seed. Monocotyledons or monocots have one cotyledon while a dicotyledon or dicot has two cotyledons. In monocot seeds, the coleorhiza is the first part to grow out of the seed, followed by the radicle. Vegetative reproduction (also vegetative propagation) is a type of asexual reproduction found in plants. It is a process by which a new plant grows or is obtained without production of seeds by the parent plant.


Why choose asexual reproduction? Perhaps the better question is: Why not? After all, asexual reproduction would seem a more efficient way to reproduce and avoids all sorts of problems. For example problems sometimes resulting from mutation as plants go through mitosis or meiosis. Another major advantage of asexual propagation is that plants can genetically be duplicated easily.


Stolons (or runners) are horizontal aboveground stems that can produce new plants from their nodes. Example is the strawberry (shown here) or grass grown in our homes and on football parks. Undergroung stems also include: rhizome, corm, bulb and tubers.


The rhizome is a modified underground stem serving as an organ of vegetative reproduction. Example is ginger. The corm is a short, solid underground stem which functions in food storage (e.g. cocoyam). Bulbs are actually large buds with a short stem enclosed by many fleshy leaves filled with stored food (e.g. onion).


Tubers are swollen tips of underground stems (e.g. potato) and root tubers have large and swollen roots (e.g. cassava). The form of vegetative reproduction called


suckers is the regeneration of a plant by shoots that arise from an existing root system. Species that characteristically produce suckers include plantains and bananas.


Some plants can also be grown by cuttings. For cuttings, a portion of a stem is cut from the parent and placed in a suitable soil. After roots have developed from the cut end of the stem, it will continue to grow. An example of this is how cassava is grown.


Another type of vegetative propagation is found in the ornamental plant Bryophyllum (also called Kalanchoë). Tiny plantlets grow along the leaf margins (when leaf fall on the ground) and later they can take up an independent existence.


Layering also involves a bending of a branch down to the ground and allowing that part of the stem to grow.


Grafting is where the tissues of one plant are encouraged to fuse with those of another. In most cases, one plant is selected for its roots, and this is called the stock or rootstock. The other plant is selected for its stems, leaves, flowers, or fruits and is called the scion.


The scion contains the desired genes to be duplicated in future production by the stock/scion plant. The scion is taken from a mature plant of the desired variety and inserted in a notch of a stump (the stock of another plant) and tied together.


For successful grafting to take place, the cambium tissues of the stock and scion plants must be placed in contact with each other and that is why it is tied. The cambium is a one–cell–thick layer of tissue between xylem and phloem in most vascular plants that is responsible for secondary growth.


After sometime, the cambiums of scion and stock are united and precautions are taken to prevent infection and drying out so that the scion will grow. It will get all its water and minerals from the root system of the stock.


Another common form of grafting is budding. A dormant side bud is cut and grafted on the stem of another stock plant, and when it has fused successfully, it is encouraged to grow.
