TRANSPORT IN MAMMALS


Blood Circulatory System

Many organisms have systems which transport materials throughout their bodies. These systems enable cells to exchange materials with the environment. Water, for example, is transported through plants in a tissue called xylem(see Transport in Plants).



But how are blood and other materials transported through the body? They are transported by the heartand blood vesselsin non–stop system which biologists call the circulatory system.


As stated, this system involves the hearta cone–shaped, muscular organ about the size of a fist and the blood vessels(arteries, veins and capillaries). We humans have got a four–chamberedheart (two atria and two ventricles) which prevents deoxygenated blood from entering the body cells.


The right and left sides of the heart are separated by an internal wall called septum. Arteriescarry blood (high in O 2; also called oxygenated blood) away from the heart toward the capillaries.


The capillariesserve as a bridge between the arteries and veins because exchange of substances (like oxygen and carbon dioxide) takes place across their thin walls. Veinsreturn blood (low in O 2; also called de–oxygenated blood) from the capillaries to the heart.


Walls of the arteries are thicker and with smaller diameters than those of the veins because they carry blood to the entire parts of the body under high pressure.

Passage of Blood through the Heart

Blood from the body enters the heartthrough the vena cava(both superior and interior) to the right atriumwhich contracts and forces the blood into the right ventricle. Contraction of the right ventricle forces the blood into pulmonary arteries.



A branch of this artery goes to each lung. In the lungs, oxygen is picked up and carbon dioxide is removed from the blood.


It is the blood from the lungs which travels through the pulmonary veinsto the left atrium. As the left atrium contracts, blood is forced into the left ventricle. A powerful contraction of the left ventricle forces blood into the aorta, the large artery from the heart.


Smaller arterieswhich branch from the aorta direct blood to all parts of the body. The arteries further divide into capillarieswhich merge to form small veins. These veins merge to form the large veins which bring blood to the right atrium.


This cardiovascular system has twocircuits. They are the pulmonary and systematic circuits. In the pulmonary circuit, pulmonary arteries take de–oxygenated blood to the lungs while the pulmonary veins return oxygenated blood to the heart.


The systematic circuittakes blood from the left ventricle of the heart (to all parts of the body) and then back to the right atrium of the heart. All this while, mention has been made of blood. What is our blood composed of?


Blood

The picture below is a sectioned blood vessel revealing that blood is composed of fourmajor components. They are red blood cells, white blood cells, platelets and plasma.



Red blood cellsas the name denotes are redand shaped like discs. They are produced in the marrow of our bones. They contain haemoglobinwhich is red in colour and therefore giving them their red colour.


They are also disc– shaped because a large surface area is needed to be able to absorb enough oxygen when the blood gets into the lungs. Oxyhaemoglobinis the term used when the haemoglobin absorbs oxygen.


It later discharges the oxygen to the cells in the body. In effect, red blood cells (also called erythrocytes) transport oxygen from the lungs to the cells in the body.


White blood cells (leukocytes)differ from red blood cells. They are usually larger, have a nucleus and lack haemoglobin.


They are also not as many as the red blood cells. White blood cells basically fight infection thereby playing a major role in our ability to resist diseases. Unlike the red blood cells, the leukocytes do not have definite shapes.


Some types of white blood cells (called phagocytes) attack bacteria in the body by engulfing it and, as it were, feed on the bacteria. Others like the lymphocytesproduce antibodies which attack and kill the bacteria. When white blood cells increase beyond normal, a cancerous disease called leukemiaresults.


Platelets (thrombocytes)are involved in the process of blood clottingor coagulationso that when one has a cut, the person does not lose all his blood.


A blood clot also does not allow bacteria to enter the body via the cut. This is what happens. When there is a cut, platelets form a compact massat the cut spot and partially seal the leak of blood.



The platelets and the injured tissues release a clotting factor called prothrombin activatorwhich changes prothrombin into thrombinwith the help of calcium ions(Ca2+). Thrombin forms long threads of amino acid chains called fibrin.


The fibrin threads wind around the cut area and provide a frameworkfor the cut. Red blood cells are the trappedin the fibrin threads (picture on the right). This makes a clot appear red.


Plasmais the liquid which accounts for about 55% of the total volume of blood with substances either dissolved or suspended in it.


Substances like water, plasma proteins, salts, gases, nutrients, etc. The plasma transports food materials needed by the body, nitrogenous wastes and hormones, the red and white blood cells and platelets.


Blood Types

Years ago, the transfusion of blood made most of its recipients die and leaving some too ill. Research was made into this and it was discovered that not alltypes of blood are compatible because of the antigensthe red blood cells tend to produce when in the recipient’s system.


As a result the ABO Systemof typing blood came to be used. The four major blood types are A, B, AB, and O. Differences in blood type involve antigens on the surface of red blood cells.

Antigensare enzymes that stimulate the production of antibodiesand that blood type is genetically determined. Note that ‘anti’ means against.Therefore anti–B (antibodies) will attack all B antigens but not A.


As an illustration, Doreen is a young woman with type A blood which contains an antibody called anti–B agglutinin( anti–Bfor short). As stated, this antibody will attack type B antigens but since Doreen has a type A blood (and therefore has no type B antigens) she is healthy.


If she goes for blood transfusion and is mistakenly given type B blood, the anti–B agglutinin (antibodies in her blood) will attack the B antigens, thus the transfused blood.


A reaction will occur making her red blood cells clumptogether. This process is called agglutination. Agglutination causes blood to stop circulating and the red blood cells to burst. In the same way, a person with type B blood will experience agglutination if blood type A is injected into his system.


People with type AB blood could receive any blood type because no clumping will occur. For this reason a person with type AB blood is called a universal recipient. A person with type O blood is called a universal donorbecause the red cells have no antigens. Type O blood cells will not be attacked by agglutinins.


Rh factors(Rh blood factors were first discovered in the rhesus monkey) are other proteins which are present on the red blood cells. Those who have the Rh factor are positive and those without it are Rh negative.


Suppose an Rh positive blood is given to a person with Rh negative blood, antibodies will be formed against the foreign blood and agglutination will result. Rh factors can harm a developing embryo if the child Rh positive and the mother is Rh negative. The mother’s blood will produce antibodies which will be foreign to the baby’s blood. This could result in anemia or death in the child. It is therefore advised that a rhesus negative woman should not marry a rhesus positive man.


Cardiovascular Disorder

Cardiovascular (involving the heart) disease is a leading cause of untimely death of most people. A few of these are discussed in this part of the topic.


Hypertension

Hypertension, referred to as high blood pressure, is a medical condition in which the blood pressure is chronically(slowly developing and long lastingly) elevated. In its current usage, the word hypertensionrefers to high blood pressure of the arteries.


Hypertension can be classified as either essential(psychologically caused) or secondary(physiologically caused). Persistent hypertension is one of the risk factorsfor strokes heart attacks and heart failure .


Strokeoften results when a small cranial artery bursts or is blocked. A lack of oxygen causes that portion of the brain to die. Paralysis(loss of the ability to move a body part) or even death may result. Heart attackoccurs when a portion of the heart muscle dies due to lack of oxygen.


Atherosclerosis

This is the accumulation of cholesterol(fat) in the arteries and it mostly begins in early adulthood. Symptoms may not appear until the individual is about 50. To prevent this it is mostly advised to eat low–fat diets, fruits and vegetables because atherosclerosis may lead to hypertension.


Lymphatic System

As noted earlier, it is at the capillaries where exchange of substances takes place. But then, not all materials are exchanged directly between body cells and the blood in the capillaries. Pressure exerted on the walls of the capillaries force some water and dissolved particles to the tissues.


This water and dissolved particles is called tissue fluid. Later some tissue fluid returns to the capillaries by osmosis. Again, not all of the tissue fluid returns. Some of the tissue fluid (that do not return) enters a special networkof lymphatic vesselsthroughout the body.


Fluid which enters the lymphatic system is called lymph.The lymphatic system consists of lymphatic vesselsand lymphoid organs. The lymphatic circulation is one–wayand so the lymphatic vessels have valves which prevent backflow of lymph.


This system does not use a special “pumping action” like the blood circulation uses the heart. It only uses random body movements which forces the lymph through the vessels to a major collecting tube, thoracic duct.


The thoracic duct empties lymph into large veins called subclavian veins(right and left). In this way, the liquid portion of the blood lost at the capillaries is restored. Lymph nodesare special structures that are scattered through the lymphatic system.
