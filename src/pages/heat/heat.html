HEAT

Before the middle of the nineteenth century, heat was thought to be an invisible, weightless substance called caloric. After many careful experiments from James Prescott Joule, he noticed that heat is a form of energy. The SI unit of heat is the Joule (J).


When two bodies of different temperatures are brought into contact, energy flows from the hotter body to the colder body until their temperatures are equal. Simply put, heat flows from a region of higher temperature to a region of lower temperature.


Sources of heat energy

Since heat is a form of energy, most of our sources of heat are examples of energy transformation (conversion of other forms of energy) into heat. The most important ones are:

  1. Solar energy; heat from the sun.

  2. Nuclear energy; heat generated from nuclear reactions.

  3. Geothermal energy; heat within the earth crust, such as molten lava from volcanoes.

  4. Friction; heat generated from resistance against sliding.

  5. Exothermic chemical reaction; this is the heat generated from chemical reaction.

  6. Burning of fuels; this is also a form of chemical reaction known as combustion.


Thermal expansion

With few exceptions, substances (solid, liquid or gases) expand when heated and contracts when cooled. When the temperature of substance is raised, its thermal energy increases. The increase in thermal energy increases the amplitude of vibrations of the atoms or molecules of the substance.


As a result, the atoms and molecules move to equilibrium positions slightly further apart than before, and the solid expands in all directions. When equal volumes of gas, liquid and solid are heated, the gas expands more than the other two and the liquid also expands than the solid.


Anomalous Expansion of Water

Most substances expand as they are being heated. Water is an exception of this general trend of heating and expansion. When water at a temperature below 0oC is heated, it expands steadily until it gets to 0oC. It begins to contract between the temperature ranges from 0oC to 4oC and expands steadily again at temperatures greater than 4oC. In that sense, water has its highest density at 4oC.


Heat Transfer

There are three modes of heat transfer. They are:

  1. Conduction is a mode of heat transfer where heat is passed on from one particle to the other without any movement of the particles.

  2. Convection is a mode of heat transfer where the heated particle of a substance moves to a different position within the substance. This mode of heat transfer is found in fluids (liquid and gases). The cooling system of an engine, ventilation of room, refrigeration, sea and land breezes works on the principle of convection currents.

  3. Radiation is a mode of heat transfer that does not require any material medium.

Land and Sea Breezes

During the day, the land gets warmer than the sea. Therefore, the air that gets in contact with the ground gets warm and then rises. Cold air from the sea comes to land to replace the rising warm air whilst air from the high atmosphere comes to replace the cold air that moved to the land. This movement of warm and cold air forms Sea Breeze.

(a) Sea breeze

Land Breeze occurs at night. At night, the land is not heated by the sun; hence it cools down faster than the sea. The air in contact with the sea gets warmer and then rises.



(b) Land Breeze

Cold air from the land moves in to take the place of the risen warm air whilst air from the high atmosphere replaces the cold air that moved to the sea. This movement also forms land breeze.


The Thermos Flask

The thermos flask is designed to maintain the temperature of its constituents. Heat losses by all the three modes of heat transfer are reduced to a possible minimum level.


Heat losses by

  1. Radiation is prevented by the silvered surface of the glass, because shiny surfaces reflect greater part of the heat it receives.

  2. Conduction and convection which require a material medium is reduced by the vacuum.


Note that bright surfaces or objects reflects greater amount of radiant heat that comes to them than a dark surface. That is why you feel the radiant heat of the sun more when you are in a dark shirt than you would when you are in a brighter one.
