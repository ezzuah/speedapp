SOLUTIONS

A solution is homogeneous mixture of two or more substances. It consists of a solute (dissolved material) and a solvent (dissolving material). A solution can be a gas (example air), liquid (example sea water) or solid (example steel). Humidity is a solution which consists of a gas phase (air), being the solvent and a liquid phase (water), being the solute.


When crystals are first dropped in a solvent, many particles may leave the crystal surface and go into solution (dissolve). As the number of solute particles in the solution increases, some of the dissolved particles will return to the surface of the crystal. With time, the number of particles leaving the surface will equal the number returning. This point is called solution equilibrium. A solution in which undissolved particles are in equilibrium with the dissolved particles is called a saturated solution. A solution containing an amount of solute less than the saturated solution is known as unsaturated solution.


The maximum amount of solute that will dissolve in 100cm3 of a solvent at a specific temperature is the solubility of the solute at that temperature. For example, a maximum of 64.2 grams of Nickel (II) Chloride will dissolve in 100cm3 of water at 20oC. The amount of solute that will dissolve to form a saturated solution depends on the temperature of the solution. The greater the temperature, the greater the amount of solute required to saturate a solution. For example, when sugar is being added to water to form sugar solution, it gets to a point where the sugar will not dissolve any longer, but will rather settle at the bottom of the container.


If the sugar solution is heated, the settled sugar will begin to dissolve. If the hot solution is cooled, an unstable solution is formed—it contains more solute than a saturated solution will normally hold. Such a solution is called a supersaturated solution. Supersaturated solution is said to be unstable because the slightest disturbance (like shaking or the presence even a small particle) will make crystals fall out of the solution.


Preparation of solution (standard)

Standard solution is a solution whose concentration is accurately known.


To prepare molar solutions (a solution with its concentration in moles/litre) first calculate the mass of the required number of moles of the solute in grams. Weigh the solute accurately with top–pan balance. Dissolve the weighed solute in a small amount of distilled water placed in a beaker. The formed solution in the beaker is then transferred into a volumetric flask. More distilled water is added to the solution to make it to the required volume.


If the concentration is given in mass per volume (e.g. grams/dm3) then, the solute is weighed accurately and dissolved in the required volume of water as explained above.

Dilution of solution

This is the process where water is added to a concentrated solution to reduce its concentration.


If a tablespoon full of sugar is dissolved in water filled to half–way of a cup, some concentration of the sugar solution has been formed. If more water is added to this solution, a new concentration has been formed. However, the amount of sugar in both solutions is the same. This means that, it is the volume that changes during dilution and this will in turn change the concentration of the solution. Note that the quantity of solute (in moles or grams) does not change when diluting a solution. This implies that:

n(initial) = n(final).

Remember that n = C x V

Since n is constant in dilutions, it follows that C(initial) x V(initial) = C(final) x V(final)
