MIXTURES AND COMPOUNDS

Most of the things around us are made up of one or more elements. For example, water is made of Hydrogen and oxygen; carbon dioxide, carbon and oxygen; table salt, sodium and chlorine, and many more. Depending on the way the constituent elements are combined, the substance can be classified as mixtures or compounds. Mixture is a substance formed from the physical combination of two or more substances (elements or compounds). On the other hand, compound is the substance formed from the chemical combination of two or more elements.


The most important thing to note is that, the elements combing chemically to form a compound do not retain their individual chemical properties. For example, oxygen as an element supports combustion. And it is present in both air and water. Air also supports combustion but water does not. This means that air is a mixture because the oxygen present in air has retained its chemical property and water is a compound because the oxygen present in water has a different chemical property from elemental oxygen.


Differences between mixtures and compounds

Compounds Mixtures
They are separated by chemical means. They are separated by physical means.
Their formation is accompanied by heat loss or gain. Their formation is not accompanied by heat loss or gain.
They have constant mass ratio of their elements. They may have varying composition.
They have entirely different properties from their constituents. The have the same properties as their constituent.
They are products of a chemical combination. They are products of a physical combination.

MIXTURES

Mixtures are grouped into the three categories below.

Solid mixtures are mixtures made up of two or more solid substances. Examples are gari and sand particles, alloys like steel (iron and carbon), brass (copper and zinc).

Liquid Mixtures are mixtures which contain two or more liquids. Examples are Chlorophyll, ethanol and water mixture.

Gaseous mixtures are mixtures which contains two or more gases. Example is air.


Mixtures can also be classified as homogeneous or heterogeneous depending on the number of phases they possess. Phase is any region of a mixture with a uniform set of properties. For example, when palm oil and water is put together, the water phase is going to have its own properties which will be different from the oil phase. It is possible to have a liquid–liquid phase as in water and palm oil, liquid–solid phase as in water and ice, Liquid–gas phase like the sea and the atmosphere, solid–gas phase like your body and the air around. The boundary between two phases is interface.


Homogeneous mixtures: These are substances that have a single phase with uniform properties. Examples are salt solution, sugar solution, air (oxygen, nitrogen, carbon –dioxide), alcohol and water, etc.


Heterogeneous mixtures: These are mixtures which have more than one phase. Example, palm oil and water; milk, made up of a fat phase and a water phase; granite is another example of a heterogeneous mixture because when it is crushed, you can pick out these three minerals, quartz, biotite and feldspar. The different phases of a heterogeneous mixture are separated from each other by definite boundaries called interfaces.


SEPARATION OF MIXTURES

It becomes necessary, at times, to separate mixtures into their original constituents. To this effect, there are various ways or methods that can be used for the separation. The physical properties of the constituents determine method to use. For example, difference in boiling points can be used in separating liquid–liquid mixtures.


Distillation

This is a physical process used to separate the constituents of a solution. The constituents of the solution must have a considerable difference in their boiling points to make the separation possible. The solution (to be separated) is heated until its temperature is between the boiling points of the two constituents. For example, the boiling points of ethanol and water are 78oC and 100oC respectively. Therefore, at 85oC only ethanol will be evaporating, leaving the water behind.



The figure above is a laboratory set up for desalination of (removal of salt from) water by distillation. Salt has a boiling point high above 100oC. Hence, the solution can be heated until the water starts boiling. The vapour from the solution will rise up through the mouth of the flask and into the condenser. The condenser consists of a small glass tube within a larger one. Cold water flows through the space between the tubes, cooling the vapour in the inner tube to condense it. The condensed water flows into the receiving flask. If the process goes on for some time, all the water will be collected in the receiving flask.

Crystallization

When a solution is heated to evaporate greater amount of the solvent, the solution then formed will contain more solute than it can hold at normal circumstance. When this solution is cooled, crystals are formed from the solution. The process of forming crystals from a supersaturated solution is called Crystallization. A supersaturated solution is a solution that contains more solute than it can dissolve.

Filtration

This is the process used to separate an insoluble solid from a solid–liquid mixture using a filter paper.


Sieving

This is the process used to separate solid–solid mixture with different particle sizes with a sieve.


Magnetisation

This is a physical process used to separate magnetic substances from non–magnetic substances using a magnet.


Precipitation

This is a chemical separation process where a substance is added to a solution which contains ionic substance for a reaction to take place to produce insoluble particles which are later filtered off. For example, barium sulfate (BaSO4) is insoluble in water; hence a solution containing barium ions (Ba2+) can be precipitated by adding a soluble sulfate such as sulfuric acid. The sulfate ions (SO42–) react with the Ba2+ to form insoluble BaSO4 that falls as precipitates. The picture below shows a precipitation process.




Pure and Impure substances

Pure substances are made up of the same kind of atoms or molecules whereas impure substances are made up of a mixture of atoms or molecules. All mixtures are classified as chemically impure whereas individual elements or compounds are chemically pure substances.
