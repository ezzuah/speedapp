LIGHT


Until the early part of the 19th century, the nature of light was explained by two conflicting theories. Sir Isaac Newton believed that light is made up of tiny particles emitted from a source and travels in a straight line. Another Dutch scientist, Christian Huygens, proposed that light is a wave.


Each scientist was able to defend his theory and that both theories were accepted. However, each theory has its own unique practical applications. In general, light is a form of energy which enables vision (seeing). The properties of light (as a particle) is summarised below.


1. Rectilinear propagation within a uniform medium.

2. Reflection.

3. Refraction.

4. Diffraction.


Each of the above property will be considered except diffraction (it is above this level).


Rectilinear Propagation Of Light


This is a property that shows that light travels in a straight line. When a small ball (particle) is rolled on a smooth surface it is expected that it moves in a straight line. Likewise, light made of particles will also travel in a straight line. This can be demonstrated by setting up three screens as shown in ‘A’ below.


Make three holes of equal radius, one in the centre of each of the three screens. Align the screens and pass a thread through them to ensure their centers are on a straight line. Remove the thread, place a source of light at one end and view through the holes from the other end (Observation 1: The light source can be seen, this means that light travels in a straight line). Shift of the screens to displace its centre from the straight line and view through the hole again (Observation 2: The light source cannot be seen). This confirms that light travel s in a straight line.


The straight path along which light travels is called a ray. A ray is represented with a straight line and an arrow pointing to the direction along which the light is traveling. A set of rays travelling together is called a light beam. The three types of beams are parallel, convergent and divergent beams (See picture below).


Sources of light

Bodies that give of light are known as luminous bodies. They are both natural and artificial luminous bodies. Example of a natural luminous body is the sun, stars, fireflies, etc. And that of artificial is electric bulbs.


Non luminous bodies do not give off light but rather, they reflect the light that falls on them into our eyes and then become visible. A body that is seen because of the light reflected from it is said to be illuminated body.


Shadows: When a ray of light falls on a body, at least one of these two phenomena might occur. Either the light goes through the body or not. If a body does not allow light to travel through it, it is referred to as an opaque body. There is another group of bodies which allow light to go through and one can see through them as well. Such bodies are said to be transparent.


Translucent bodies allow light to travel through but one can not see through them. A shadow is a dark image formed on a screen when an opaque object is placed between a light source and a screen. The two types of shadows are umbra (total darkness) or penumbra (partial darkness). The pictures in ‘A’ and ‘B’ below are shadows formed from a point and extended (larger) light sources respectively. Point light sources have only umbra and extended light sources have both umbra and penumbra.



Eclipses We know from our study of the Solar System that the earth revolves around the sun, and the moon also revolves around the earth. When light from the sun is blocked from reaching the earth or the moon we say an eclipse has occurred. The two types of eclipse are eclipse of the sun and eclipse of the moon. Eclipse of the sun, also known as solar eclipse, occurs when the moon comes between the sun and the earth, and a shadow of the moon is cast on the earth.



This shadow has both umbra and penumbra (see picture above). The region of the umbra is said to be experiencing total eclipse (total darkness) and that of the penumbra is partial eclipse (partial darkness).


People in the umbra do not see any part of the sun, but, those in the umbra do (see picture above). Eclipse of the moon (lunar eclipse), on the other hand, occurs when the earth comes between the sun and the moon and blocks the sun rays from reaching the moon (See the picture below).
