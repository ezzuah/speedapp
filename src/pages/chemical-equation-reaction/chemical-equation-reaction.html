CHEMICAL REACTIONS AND EQUATIONS


What happens to the gas in a gas cylinder when a gas stove is lighted? Chemist analysed the process and realized that the gas contains a substance called methane (CH4). This substance combines with oxygen (O2) in the air to form carbon dioxide (CO2) and water (H2O)–vapour. In the picture below, the chemical formulas of the named compounds have been combined in a certain pattern to represent the chemical change that occurs.



Chemical reaction is the process by which one or more substances are changed into one or more other substances. The equation written to represent the chemical change that occurred in a chemical reaction is called a chemical equation. To write an equation that will accurately represent a chemical reaction, you perform these three things


  1. Determine the reactants and the products. Taking the figure above for instace, the reactants are CH4 and O2 whilst the products are CO2 and H2O.


  2. Group the reactants on one side (mostly left) and the products on another side (right) after which you link them with an arrow pointing to the products.


  3. Balance the equation. This means show the same number of atoms of each element on both the reactants and products sides. The equation under the picture above is an example of a balanced chemical eqaution. The numbers of the various atoms on each side of the equation are equal.


Note that the alphabet ‘g’ in parenthesis indicates that the substances are gases. Solid state is represented by ‘s’, liquid stated by ‘l’ and ‘aq’ for aqueous state. Aqueous simply means in solution. For example, NaCl disolved in water will be represented as NaCl(aq).


Balancing chemical equation

There is no rule to follow in balacing chemical equations. However, keeping either single or even coefficients makes the balancing relatively easy in most problems.


Example 1

Sodium reacts with water to form sodium hydroxide and hydrogen gas. Write and balance a chemical equations for this reaction.


Solution


Sodium = Na


Water = H2O


Sodium hydroxide = NaOH(refer to lesson 5 for better understanding)


Hydrogen gas = H2



Grouping reactants and products:


Na + H2O NaOH + H2


Na = 1                       Na = 1


H = 2                       H = 3 (1 atom in NaOH and 2 in Hydrogen gas)


O = 1                       O = 1


Putting 2 in front of NaOH to get an even number for the H atoms yields 4 H atoms and 2 O atoms on the left.


Na + H2O 2NaOH + H2


Na = 1                       Na = 2


H = 2                       H = 4


O = 1                       O =2




Now, balancing the O by putting 2 before H2O


Na + 2H2O 2NaOH + H2


Na = 1                       Na = 2; not balanced.


H = 4                       H = 4 (2 in NaOH and 2 in H2 meaning it is balanced)


O = 2                       O =2 (O is also balanced)


Putting 2 before Na balances the equation


2Na + 2H2O 2NaOH + H2


Na = 2                       Na = 2 (balanced)


H = 4                       H = 4 (balanced)


O = 2                       O =2 (O balanced)



Example 2

Balance the equation below.


CaO + NH4Cl CaCl + NH3 + H2O


Ca = 1                            Ca = 1


O = 1                            O = 1


N = 1                            N = 1


H = 4                            H = 5 (3 in NH3 and 2 in H2O)


Cl = 1                            Cl = 2



Putting 2 before NH3 to make the coefficient of H even yields


CaO + NH4Cl CaCl2 + 2NH3 + H2O


Ca = 1                            Ca = 1


O = 1                            O = 1


N = 1                            N = 2 (in NH3)


H = 4                            H = 8 (6 in NH3 and 2 in H2O)


Cl = 1                            Cl = 2


Now, balancing the N by putting 2 before NH4Cl


CaO + 2NH4Cl CaCl2 + 2NH3 + H2O


Ca = 1                            Ca = 1


O = 1                            O = 1


N = 2                            N = 2 (balanced)


H = 8 (in NH4Cl)                            H = 8 (balanced)


Cl = 2                            Cl = 2 (balanced)

The equation is now balanced.


Types of chemical reactions

  1. Direct combination; element + element Compound


  2. Decomposition; compounds 2 or more elements or compounds


  3. Single replacement; element + compound element + compound


  4. Double replacement; compound + compound compound + compound


Chemical reaction can be classified as homogeneous (when all the reactants are in the same physical state) and heterogeneous (when the reactants are NOT in the same physical state)
