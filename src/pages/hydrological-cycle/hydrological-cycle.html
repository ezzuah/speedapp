HYDROLOGICAL CYCLE


Water is essential to life. Without it, the biosphere would fail to exist. But the largest reservoir is the collection of oceans, accounting for 97% of the Earth's water, which is toxic to humans and many plants and animals. The next largest quantity (2%) is stored in solid form in the ice caps and glaciers. How do we obtain fresh water resources then? Where does drinkable water come from? To understand, we need to turn to the Hydrological (water) Cycle. Water has many unique properties that allow it to be such a universal material.


One special characteristic of water is its ability to change state very easily under Earth conditions. It can be found readily on the planet in all of its three forms, solid, liquid, and gas. These forms also play a great part in the hydrological cycle. Now, exactly what is the hydrologic cycle? The hydrological cycle describes the continuous movement of water on, above, and below the surface of the Earth. Since the water cycle is truly a "cycle," there is no beginning or end.


The hydrologic cycle takes place in the hydrosphere—this is the region containing all the water in the atmosphere and on the surface of the earth. The cycle is the movement of water through this hydrosphere. We can think of this cycle as the earth’s water budget. But note that in whatever ‘budget’ the water cycle is doing, there is always the same amount of water on the surface of the earth.



You can refer to the picture concurrently as you read about the cycle. The sun, which drives the water cycle, heats water in the oceans. Some of it evaporates as vapour into the air. Ice and snow can sublimate directly into water vapor. Rising air currents take the vapor up into the atmosphere, along with water from evapotranspiration, which is water transpired from plants and evaporated from the soil. The vapor rises into the air where cooler temperatures cause it to condense into clouds.


Air currents move clouds around the globe; cloud particles collide, grow, and fall out of the sky as precipitation. Some precipitation falls as snow and can accumulate as ice caps and glaciers, which can store frozen water for thousands of years. Snow in warmer climates often melts, and the melted water flows overland. Most precipitation falls back into the oceans or onto land, where, due to gravity, the precipitation flows over the ground as surface runoff. A portion of runoff enters rivers in valleys in the landscape, with stream flow moving water towards the oceans. Runoff, and ground–water seepage, accumulate and are stored as freshwater in lakes. Not all runoff flows into rivers. Much of it soaks into the ground as infiltration. Some water infiltrates deep into the ground and replenishes subsurface rocks, which store huge amounts of freshwater for long periods of time. Some infiltration stays close to the land surface and can seep back into surface–water bodies (and the ocean) as ground–water discharge. Some ground water finds openings in the land surface and emerges as freshwater springs. Over time, the water continues flowing, some to reenter the ocean, where the water cycle renews itself.


The saying that one can live longer without food than without water tells us how important water is and for that matter the hydrological cycle is to all living things. Apart from individual use (e.g. 83% of the human circulatory system is water; drinking, respiration, perspiration, and elimination of wastes), large amounts of water are needed for most economic activities: agriculture and mining, food processing, manufacturing (e.g., about 120,000 liters of water are used in the production of an average automobile), and also in the generation of electricity even here in Ghana.


You can still think of more! The hydrological cycle is indeed a functional prerequisite for all living organisms. Despite these established facts, the hydrological cycle still gets polluted by man, leading to consequences which are harmful to organisms and plants which live on or in these water bodies. The primary sources of water pollution are generally grouped into two categories based on their point of origin. Point–source pollution refers to contaminants that enter a waterway through a discrete "point source".


Examples of this category include discharges from a wastewater treatment plant, outfalls from a factory, leaking underground tanks, etc. The second primary category, non–point source pollution, refers to contamination that does not originate from a single discrete source. Runoff water from an agricultural field, or metals and hydrocarbons from an area with high impervious surfaces and vehicular traffic are examples of non–point source pollution.


For example in the early 1900s, before the massive use of chlorine, pesticides, herbicides and the tens of thousands other chemicals that we are exposed to today, the average person had a 1 in 50 chance of getting cancer. But today, 1 in 3 can expect to get cancer in their lifetime. Pathogens in water bodies can produce diseases in either human or animal hosts.


With all the above in mind, you will bear with me that the little water we have got on the earth should not be wasted but be conserved. Water conservation refers to reducing use of fresh water, through technological or social methods. What methods can you think of which will help conserve water in your home? What about in a factory?
