CHANGE OF STATE


The molecular formula for ice block, liquid water and water vapour is H2O. They all have the same molecular formula, although, they exist in different physical states. What do you think might be causing this? The physical state a substance assumes or may assume depends on the temperature and pressure it is subjected to. We will narrow our discursion on this topic to temperature only.


Melting and Freezing: When sugar is heated to a certain temperature it melts or fuse. The temperature at which a substance melts is its melting point. At the melting temperature, any heat applied to the sugar goes in to break the bonds which are holding its molecules in fixed positions, causing it to melt (flow). It is after all the sugar particles have been melted, that its temperature will begin to rise again.


When the melted sugar is cooled (heat is removed from it), its temperature will be reducing until it gets to the melting point. At the melting point, the temperature remains constant and the sugar solidifies. The melting point is the same as the freezing point. Freezing point is the temperature at which a substance changes state from liquid to solid. This means that melting and freezing of a particular substance occur at the same temperature. An example is supplying heat to ice at 0oC will cause it to melt and removing heat from water at that same 0oC will freeze it. The presence of impurities lowers the freezing point of liquids.


Boiling and Condensation: Using a similar rule of thought for the Melting and Freezing, we can come to the conclusion that boiling and condensation occur at the same temperature. For example, liquid water boils at 100oC when it is heated whilst at that same temperature, water vapour will condense when heat is removed from it.


The following factors affect boiling point diversely.


  1. The presence of impurities increases the boiling point.


  2. Reduced atmospheric pressure reduces the boiling point.


  3. Less dense liquids have a lower boiling point than denser liquids.


Evaporation: When some amount of robbing alcohol is placed in a shallow dish at normal room temperature, you will notice that the level of the alcohol decreases in a short time and the odor of alcohol become strong in the air around. We say the alcohol has evaporated.


Evaporation is the process through which liquid molecules absorbs heat from its surrounding and escape to the gaseous state. Evaporation causes cooling effect. For example, when you dip your finger in a robbing alcohol and allow air to blow over it, the finger feels cool. The alcohol absorbs heat from the hand as it is evaporating; this makes your hand feel cold. The human body employs evaporation to cool down. If you sweat on a hot sunny day, the sweat takes some heat from your body as it evaporates and your body cools down.


The rate of evaporation is increased by the following.


  1. Increasing the surface area of the liquid in contact with (or exposed to) air.


  2. Lowering the pressure on the liquid.


  3. Increasing the temperature of the liquid.


  4. Lower the relative humidity of the surroundings.


  5. An increase in wind velocity increases the rate of evaporation.


Differences between boiling and evaporation
Boiling Evaporation

Sublimation: Just like robbing alcohol, a piece of camphor left in the open air becomes smaller and smaller until it disappears eventually. The molecules of the camphor escape from the solid surface and go into the gaseous state. Sublimation is the process through which a substance changes state from the solid state to the gaseous state.


Sensible and latent hea

Sensible Heat This is the heat energy that changes the temperature of a substance without changing its state when added or removed. For example, the temperature of water at 20oC will rise if heat is applied to it. This heat applied to the water at that temperature is sensible heat.


Heat Capacity This is the heat energy required to raise the temperature of a substance through a temperature difference of 1oC. Heat capacity tells very little about the thermal properties of a substance. For example, the energy required to raise the temperature of 2g of aluminum through 1oC is different from that required to raise the temperature of 3g of aluminum or 4g of copper through that same 1oC temperature range.


For the bases of comparism, equal mass or weight of the substances must be used. Scientist therefore defined specific heat capacity which is a better quantity for comparism. The specific heat capacity of a substance is the energy required to raise the temperature of a unit mass (1 Kg) of that substance through 1oC or 1K. The amount of sensible heat added or removed from a body can be calculated from the formula below.


Q = m x c x (T2–T1)

Where Q = heat added, m = mass of substance, T1 = initial temperature and T2 = final temperature

Latent Heat; this is the heat energy that does not change the temperature of a substance but rather, changes its physical state when it is added or removed. If heat is added to liquid water at a temperature of 100oC, the temperature of the liquid does not change. However, the water molecules absorb the energy added and as a result, they evaporate or move from liquid state to gaseous state. There are two forms of latent heats and they are:


  1. Latent Heat of Fusion; this is the energy required to change the state of a substance from solid to liquid state at its normal melting point. The specific latent heat of fusion of a substance is the energy required to change the state of 1kg of that substance from solid to liquid at its normal melting point.


  2. Latent Heat of Vapourisation; this is the heat required to change the state of a substance from liquid to gas at its normal boiling point. The specific latent heat of Vapourisation of a substance is the energy required to change the state of 1kg of that substance from liquid to gas at its normal boiling point.


Note that the heat required to melt a substance is the same as the heat required to solidify it. For example, if 5g of solid water (ice) will need a heat of 21J to melt it completely then, a heat of 21J should be taken away from water at a temperature of 0oC to solidify it. In simple words, latent heat of fusion is the same for both freezing and melting. The only difference being that, heat is added during melting whilst it is removed during solidification.


Similarly, latent heat of vapourisation of a substance is the same for both vapourising and condensing. Heat is added during vapourising whereas it is removed during condensation.
