PHYSICAL PROPERTIES OF MATTER

Matter is defined as any substance that has mass and occupies space (volume). Matter can be classified into three broad categories based on the strength of the forces holding their particles together. These are solids, liquids and gases (refer from bonding). Aside the three states of matter, we shall discuss other properties like mass, weight, length density and many more which are common to all forms of matter.


Density

‘Stone is heavier than wood’ they say. This is not so meaningful. A cubic meter of wood is heavier than a cubic centimeter of stone and the other way round is also true.


To make such statement meaningful, one must use equal volumes of stone and wood for such comparism. The scientist uses density for such comparisms. Density is defined as the mass per unit volume of a substance.

Floatation

If a body is denser than a liquid, it sinks in it completely. However, if the density of the body is lesser than that of the liquid in which it is placed, the body will sink until the mass of the liquid displaced by the portion of the body submerged under the liquid is equal to the mass of the body.


The principle of floatation states that a floating body displaces its own weight of the liquid in which it floats. Objects denser than water, even though they sink readily, appear to lose part of their weight when submerged.


Archimedes’ Principle

Archimedes noticed that liquids exert an upward force known as upthrust on bodies that are dropped in them. The figure below shows an overflow can which was filled with water to the spout.



A stone with weight Ws is dropped in the overflow can. A catch bucket is used to collect the water that is displaced by the stone. Note that the volume of water collected in the catch bucket is equal to the volume of the stone.


The magnitude of the upthrust the water exerts on the stone is equal to the weight of the displaced water which is Ww. Note that the volume of stone and the displaced water are equal but their masses are not.


Therefore, the stone will sink because its density is greater than that of water. Archimedes’ principle states that, a body immersed in a liquid, wholly or partly, loses weight. The weight loss is equal to the weight of the liquid displaced by the body.  

The picture below shows two spring balances with threads tied to two crowns. One crown is weighed in air and the other in water. The one weighed in water losses weight which is equal to the upthrust exerted on it by the water.



Finally, the condition for a body to float in a liquid is that its weight should be less or equal to the upthrust exerted on it by the liquid in which it is plunged.


Application of floatation

  1. Ships float because their average density is less than the density of water. In other words, the weight of the volume displaced by the ship is greater than weight of the ship.


  2. The turbines of an aircraft displaces huge amount of air whose weight is greater than the weight of the aircraft and this makes it buoyant.


  3. The submarine sinks and floats because water is pumped in and out of its ballast tanks respectively.


  4. Helium filled balloons rises in air because their density is less than the density of air.


Cohesion and Adhesion

The force of attraction between particles of the same substance is termed cohesion. For example, if you have a drop of water at your finger tip, and you lower it to the surface of a glass of water, cohesive forces attracts the drop on your finger tip to the water in the glass.


Adhesion is the force of attraction between particles of different substances. For example, chewing gum sticks to shoe leather, glue sticks to wood and many more. When two substances are in contact, both cohesion and adhesion exist at the contact surface.


These forces determine the behaviour of the substances at the contact surface. For example, when chalk and stone are scratched at the surface of a black board, some of the chalk particles will adhere to the surface of the board but stone will not.


This means that the adhesive force between the chalk and the board is greater than the cohesive force in chalk. And the adhesive force between the stone and the board is less than the cohesive force in stone.


Liquids which have strong cohesive forces do not wet surfaces of substances (e.g. mercury) and those with weak cohesive forces wet the surfaces of substances (ethanol). This property of matter is found in solids and liquids only.


Surface Tension

Have you ever poured a drink into a glass such that the surface of the drink is higher than the rim of the glass? You will realize the drink does not over flow the glass although its surface is slightly higher than the rim of the glass.


Needle or razor blade can also float in water if they are placed carefully on the surface, even though they are seven times as dense as water. These scenarios are clear demonstrations of surface tension in liquids. Surface tension is the attraction of the surface layer–molecules to themselves forming a thin covering over the liquid.

Surface tension holds a water strider on the water surface (left). Soap bubbles form as a result of surface tension (middle). Water droplet rolls into spherical shape due to surface tension (right). Umbrellas and rain coats are effective because of surface tension. This property is found in only liquids.


Capillarity

This is the rise or fall of liquids in tubes with small inner diameters. For example, water rises in a small capillary tube due to capillarity. Capillarity depends on adhesion and surface tension. Adhesion of water causes the water to creep up the glass walls. This will give the water a concave surface. Surface tension then reduces the concaveness of the water surface by flattening the surface. The flattening surface indicates the water level in the tube will rise. Repeated creeping and flattening will eventually raise the water level in the tube. Kerosene rises up the wick of a lantern through capillarity. This property is limited to liquids only.
