ESTERS (Alkylalkanoates)

It was mentioned earlier under the reactions of alcohols that esters are formed from a reaction between a carboxylic acid and an alcohol. Esters made from alcohols and organic acids of low molecular mass are colourless liquids that have agreeable fruity ordour. Many of the odours in flowers come from esters. Esters are therefore used in the preparation of perfumes and synthetic flavors. Methyl acetate gives nail polish its odour. Esters can also be formed when an inorganic acid reacts with an alcohol.


Nomenclature

They are named by considering them as derivative of a carboxylic acid (alkanoate) and alcohol (alkyl). The name is thus obtained by combining the derivatives of the acid and alcohol. The alkyl group of the alcohol is named first, followed by the alkanoate group from the acid. The first example is an ester formed from methanol and propanoic acid.

Uses of Esters

They are used as solvents in quick–drying paints and lacquers.

They are also used as flavors or fragrance in food preparations and cosmetics.

Soaps and Detergent

Soaps and detergents are cleansing agents. Ordinary soaps are sometimes referred to as soapy detergents whereas detergents such as washing powders are referred to as soapless detergents. In this lesson, we will refer to soapy detergents as soaps and soapless detergents as detergents. From a chemical point of view, soaps are metallic salts of a higher carboxylic acid. They are made by boiling oils or fats with alkali solutions of NaOH or KOH. During the heating process, a chemical reaction, saponification, takes place between the ester (fats or oil) and the base (NaOH or KOH) which forms soap and glycerol. Saponification is a chemical reaction between a hot concentrated alkali and an ester to form soap and glycerol.


Saponification:

Fat/oil + Conc. Alkali heat Soap + Glycerol


(C17H35COO)3C3H5 + 3NaOH 3C17H35COONa + C3H5(OH)3 + Heat


From the above explanation, we realize that soap is made from oil which is insoluble in water and a water–soluble base (alkali). In short, soap has a water–insoluble part coming from the ester which is known as hydrophobic part and water–soluble part coming from the alkali, known as hydrophilic part. When the alkali used is NaOH, the soap formed is hard and does not lather so easily. Such soaps are called hard soap. However, if the alkali used is made from KOH, the soap formed is soft and lathers easily. Soaps formed from potassium hydroxide (KOH) are therefore referred to as soft soaps. Each kind of soaps has its own advantages and hence serves better in different areas. Hard soaps are good for laundry purposes due to their hard nature. And soft soaps are good for bathing. Examples of soap include key soap, guardian, rexona, imperial leather, etc.


Detergents are synthetic soaps. They are sodium or potassium salts of a long chain organic tetraoxosulphate (IV) acid or sulphonic acids. Their structure is similar to soaps. They have soluble and insoluble parts just like soaps. They are made from sulphonic acid and an alkali. Examples of detergents include omo, so klin, ariel, yazz, and proper washing powder.


Cleansing Action of Soaps and Detergents

All forms of stains are chemicals. These chemicals may be organic (grease), inorganic (clay mud) or both. When soap is applied to the stain in the presence of water, the soap breaks down into the soluble and insoluble parts through lathering. The water–soluble parts attacks and dissolves inorganic stains whilst the water–insoluble parts attacks the organic stains. Agitation by the hand or washing machine removes the stains and leaves them suspend in the water as small (colloidal) particles. Detergent acts just like soap. However, it has this advantage over soap, it does not form scum in hard water. Detergents are not biodegradable, can not be broken down by the action of micro–organisms, and that limits their uses somehow. Soaps are biodegradable.


Preparation of Soap by Saponification (Alkaline Hydrolysis)

Equal masses of groundnut oil and sodium hydroxide pellets are taken, say 10g each. The pellets are dissolved in 40g of water (note: the mass of water is four times that of pellets). Pour the oil and the alkali into a beaker. Heat and stir the mixture gently until it boils for about 5 minutes. Add water when needed to maintain the level of the mixture as it being heated. After the mixture has boiled for 5 minutes, allow it to cool a little. Add about 40g of saturated sodium chloride solution (brine) and stir. The soap is not very soluble in the brine. The soap therefore separates from the mixture and rises to the top as a solid layer. This process of separating the soap from the mixture is called salting out. Skim or filter the solid product, soap, and wash it with some water. Place the soap between two filter papers and press to obtain solid soap.
