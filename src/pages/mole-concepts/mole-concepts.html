MOLE CONCEPT

The mole is a number like the dozen. We can say a dozen of eggs, a dozen of oranges, a dozen of chlorine atoms etc. The mole is equal to 6.02 X 1023 particles (atoms, ions or molecules). This number is what we call the Avogadro's constant and it is denoted by NA.


The mole is also in reference with the carbon-12 element. 12 grams of carbon-12 element contains 6.02 X 1023 atoms. This means that 1 mole of any substance (elements or compounds) contains 6.02 X 1023 particles. For instance, 1 mole of water contains 6.02 X 1023 of water molecules. The mole is the amount of a substance that contains as many particles as there are in 12 grams of carbon-12 element (which is 6.02 X 1023 particles). It is denoted by n.


The mass of 1 mole of a substance (atoms, ions or molecules) is called the Molar mass. It is denoted by M and its unit is grams per mole(g/mol). If the mass of 1 mole of carbon-12 is 12 grams, then sodium which has an atomic mass of 23u will also have a molar mass of 23 grams. (Reason: the mass of one atom of carbon-12 is 12u and the molar mass is 12 grams). If the mass of 1 atom of an element is say 74 u, then its molar mass will be 74 g/mol.

Mole Concept Applied to Concentraion

Concentration can be expressed in a number of ways. Let us consider the dissolution of cube sugar in water to form a uniform solution. The cubes could be counted and dissolved to form the solution. The concentration in such a case can be expressed as 5 cubes per a litre of water, 5 dozens of cubes per a litre of water or 5 moles of cubes per litre of water. If the concentration is in mole per a litre of water, then such a concentration is called the molar concentration or molarity. Molar concentration is defined as the number of moles of solute dissolved in a litre of solvent.


The formula for molar concentration is given as:

Cn = n
V 		; mol/l or mol/dm3or M

Where Cn is the molar concentration,

n is the number of mole dissolved in the solvent and

V is the volume of solvent.


There is another instance where the mass of the cubes is taken before dissolving in water. The concentration is then expressed as grams per litre or kilograms per litre. This kind of concentration is known as the mass concentration.


The formula for mass concentration

Cm = m(mass of solute)
V (Volume of solvent) 		; grams/litre or grams/dm3


Where Cm is the mass concentration, m is the mass of solute dissolved in the solvent and V is the volume of solvent.


Finally, concentration can also be expressed in percentages. For example if 15 litres of ethanol is dissolved in 85 litres of water, then the percentage by volume of ehtanol in the solution is 15%. Therefore the concentration can be written as 15% (v/v), (v/v) means volume per volume. Similarly, concentration can be expressed as (w/w), that is weight per weight. Another form of concentration is part per million (ppm). If 1 mg of solute is dissolved in 1 kg of solvent, then the concentration is obtained is 1 ppm. Note that 1,000,000 mg = 1000 g = 1 kg

Mole concept applied to chemical reaction

Let us consider the reaction between hydrogen and oxygen to form water.

H2 + O2 H2O ;

Note that the equation is not balanced.


The reactants in the picture below is made up of 10H2 and 7O2 and the products is 10 H2O and 2O2. Each oxygen atom will combine with 2 hydrogen atoms to form water. It follows that the 7O2 requires 14 H2 to form 14 H2O. However, since there are only 10H2 present, only 5O22will then react completely with the 10 H2. Hydrogen is called the limiting reactant because its quantity is not sufficient to react with all the 7O2 molecules present.

H2 O2 H2O
2 molecules 1 molecule 2 molecules
2 moles 1 mole 2 moles
1 mole 0.5 mole 1 mole

It is clear from the above deduction that the balance equation is


2H2 + O2 2 H2O

The equation is balanced.


This means that, the ratio with which elements combine or the coefficient written before elements in a balanced equation can be seen as the moles required for complete reaction between the two elements where there will be no limiting reactant.
