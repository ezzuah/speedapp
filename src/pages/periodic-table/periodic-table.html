THE PERIODIC TABLE

Your school has an academic time table, and you might probably have your personal time table. Supposing the schedules on these time table were written in statement form, do you think reading would have been that easy? Scietists have, so far, discovered 109 elements. Some of the elements show similar behaviour (property).


For example, sodium reacts vigorously with water. Potassium also reacts vigorously with water. Both elements are soft and have shiny surface. Later experimints revealed that both elements have one outer electron which is responsible for their similar properties. Certain electron arrangements repeat themselve as atomic number increases and this has brought about similarities in elemental properties.


Scientists have therefore arranged all the elements in a table known as the periodic table such that in a glance one can predict the behaviour of an element base on its electronic structure. The periodic table has (horizontal) rows called periods and (vertical) columns called groups.



Elements are basically classified as metals, non-metals or semi-metals on the table.


Periods

Elements on a particular period have the same number of shells. Example is hydrogen and helium as shown in the picture below. The atomic numbers of adjacent atoms increase by one unit when you move from left to right on a period. For example, nitrogen and oxygen are on the same period and their respective atomic numbers are 7 and 8.


The periods are assigned numbers which is equal to the number of shells within the atoms (of element) that occupy them. For example, you would find hydrogen which has a single shell on period 1, oxygen (see next page for structure) with two shells on period 2 and sulphur with three shells on period 3. There are seven periods in all and as you move down the table, the number of shells increases by one from one period to the next.




Groups

There are 8 groups excluding the transition metals. Elements in the same group have the same number of valence electrons. Since valence electrons are responsible for the chemical properties of an element, it implies that elements in the same group will have similar chemical properties because they have the same number of valence electrons. Like the periods, the groups have been assigned numbers. The number assigned to a group is equal to the number of valence electrons atoms (of elements) in that group possesses. The figure below shows fluorine and chlorine respectively; they have the same number of valence electrons (7) so they are placed in the same group (group 7).




At this point, let us consider the properties of groups 1, 2, 7 and 8 elements.


Group 1 (Lithium, Sodium, Potassium, etc.)

These elements are also known as alkali metals. They have single electron in their valence shell. They are very reactive. They react by loosing their only valence electron thereby forming a +1 ion always.

* They tarnish in air as results of their reaction with oxygen to form oxides. Therefore, they are mostly kept in paraffin.

* They displace (release) hydrogen from its compounds in the form hydrogen gas.

* They are good conductors of heat and electricity .

* They have low melting point, boiling point and density as compared to other metals.


Note that Hydrogen has a valence electron of one, just like the alkali metals; it can react by loosing its valence electron and form a +1 ion. Unlike alkali metals, hydrogen can also accept or share electrons. Because of this unique property, hydrogen is considered as a family on its own.


Group 2 (Beryllium, Magnesium, Calcium, etc)

The family name of this group is the Alkaline earth metals. These elements are found in group two (next to the alkali metal). They have two valence electrons just like helium. However, they are not placed in the same column with helium. This is so because, the two valence electrons of helium fills the K shell fully (refer from Atomic structure) which is not the case for the alkaline earth metals. They react by loosing the two outer electrons, forming +2 ions always. They have relatively high melting point, boiling point and density.


Group 7 (Fluorine, Chlorine, Bromine, Iodine, etc)

The family name of this group is the halogens. They are very reactive. They have seven valence electrons so they react by accepting one electron to complete their valence shell and acquire a –1 charge. Fluorine and chlorine are gases, bromine is a volatile liquid and iodine is a solid that sublimes easily.


Group 8 (Helium, Neon, Argon, Krypton, Xenon and Radon)

These elements are called inert or noble gases. They all have 8 electrons in their valence shell except helium, which has 2. From the family name, ‘inert’ and ‘gases’, it means they are inactive or unreactive and exist as gases. Because they already have a stable structure, they exist as monoatomic or single gas particles.
