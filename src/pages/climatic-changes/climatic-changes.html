ATMOSPHERE AND CLIMATE CHANGE


Whenever you hear the word ‘atmosphere’ what meaning do you give to it? You may understand the ‘sphere’ but what about the other? The word was coined from the Greek ‘atmos’ (meaning vapour) sphaira (sphere).


By definition, an atmosphere is a layer of gases that may surround a material body of sufficient mass, by the gravity of the body, and are retained for a longer duration if gravity is high and the atmosphere's temperature is low.


We are going to learn, in this topic, about the atmosphere and the changes we experience in our climate. The earth's atmosphere contains oxygen used by most organisms for respiration and carbon dioxide used by plants and algae for photosynthesis. It also protects living organisms from genetic damage by the sun’s ultraviolet radiation.


Its current composition is the product of billions of years of biochemical modification by living organisms. The atmosphere consists, from the ground up, of the troposphere (which includes the planetary boundary layer or peplosphere as lowest layer), stratosphere, mesosphere, ionosphere (or thermosphere), exosphere and the magnetosphere. Each of the layers has a different lapse rate (the rate of change in temperature with height).


The troposphere begins at the Earth's surface. This is to means three quarters of the atmosphere lies within the troposphere, and the depth of this layer varies between 17 km at the equator and 7 km at the poles.


This layer acts as a source of heat resulting from absorption of visible sunlight. The temperature decreases with height in the troposphere, and so the air is well mixed in this region. Weather phenomena such as thunderstorms and clouds occur in this layer.


About 6 to 10 to 17 kilometers above the Earth, a new region called the stratosphere begins. The stratosphere (Latin: stratum, layer) as its meaning denotes contains the "ozone layer". It is heated from above (absorption of solar ultraviolet radiation by oxygen and ozone) and temperature increases with altitude. At about 50 kilometers, temperature begins to



decrease with altitude again and the mesosphere begins. The Kármán line, located within the thermosphere at an altitude of 100 km, is commonly used to define the boundary between the Earth's atmosphere and outer space.


However, the exosphere can extend from 500 up to 10,000 km above the surface, where it interacts with the planet's magnetosphere. Both the stratosphere and the troposphere have important direct and indirect effects on the well–being of humankind.


In this century, it has become increasingly clear that humans are influencing the chemical composition of the troposphere and stratosphere in ways that can impact conditions at the Earth's surface. Some of the most challenging environmental issues of our time have arisen —ozone layer depletion and green house effect.


Ozone depletion describes a slow, steady decline of about 4 percent per decade in the total amount of ozone in Earth's stratosphere since the late 1970s; and a much larger, but seasonal, decrease in stratospheric ozone over Earth's Polar Regions during the same period commonly referred to as the ozone hole.


The main source of these depleting agents is chlorofluorocarbon (CFC) compounds. CFCs and other contributory substances are commonly referred to as ozone–depleting substances (ODS).


Since the ozone layer prevents most harmful ultraviolet light from passing through the Earth's atmosphere, observed and projected decreases in ozone have generated worldwide concern leading to adoption of the Montreal Protocol banning the production of CFCs as well as related ozone depleting chemicals such as carbon tetrachloride and trichloroethane.


It is suspected that a variety of biological consequences such as increases in skin cancer, damage to plants, and reduction of plankton populations in the oceans may result from the increased ultraviolet exposure due to ozone depletion.


Global warming is the increase in the average measured temperature of the Earth's near–surface air and oceans since the mid–20th century, and its projected continuation.


So far the scientific consensus is that the increase in atmospheric greenhouse gases is due to human activity caused and this began since the start of the industrial era (beginning in the mid 1700s).


Human activity since the industrial revolution has increased the concentration of various gases, leading to increased radiative forcing from CO2, methane, ozone, CFCs and nitrous oxide. The present atmospheric concentration of CO2 is about 385 parts per million (ppm) by volume. Future CO2 levels are expected to rise due to ongoing burning of fossil fuels and land–use change.


Fossil fuels are fuels consisting of high content of carbon. Increasing global temperature is expected to cause sea levels to rise, an increase in the intensity of extreme weather events, and significant changes to the amount and pattern of precipitation (e.g. raining, snowing, etc), likely leading to an expanse of tropical areas and increased pace of desertification.


Other expected effects of global warming include changes in agricultural yields, modifications of trade routes, glacier retreat or melting of ice (glaciated) areas which means a worldwide sea level rise, mass species extinctions and increases in the ranges of disease vectors.


Because of this, most national governments have signed and signed the Kyoto Protocol aimed at reducing greenhouse gas emissions, but there is ongoing political and public debate worldwide regarding what, if any, action should be taken to reduce or reverse future warming or to adapt to its expected consequences. Human emissions of sulphur and nitrogen compounds react in the atmosphere to produce acids. These acids are absorbed to form clouds and later the clouds fall as rain. But the composition of the clouds was acids so the rain is called an acid rain.


Acid rain also comprises of any other form of precipitation that is unusually acidic e.g. fog, mist, dew, snow, etc. the principal causes, as stated, are from human sources, such as electricity generation, factories, and motor vehicles. Coal power plants are one of the most polluting.


The gases can be carried hundreds of kilometers in the atmosphere before they are converted to acids and deposited. In the past, factories had short funnels to let out smoke, but this caused many problems locally; thus, factories now have taller smoke funnels. However, dispersal from these taller stacks causes pollutants to be carried farther, causing widespread ecological damage.


Acid rain has been shown to have adverse impacts on forests, freshwaters and soils, killing insect and aquatic life forms as well as causing damage to buildings and having impacts on human health.


Places such as much of South Asia (Indonesia, Malaysia and Thailand), Western part of South Africa, Southern India and Sri Lanka and even West Africa (countries like Ghana, Togo and Nigeria) could all be prone to acidic rainfall in the future. What is the way forward?


A wet scrubber has been invented for use by coal–burning power plants. A wet scrubber is basically a reaction tower equipped with a fan that extracts hot smoke stack gases from a power plant into the tower.


Lime or limestone in suspension form is also injected into the tower to mix with the stack gases and combine with the sulphur dioxide present.


The calcium carbonate of the limestone produces pH–neutral calcium sulphate that is physically removed from the scrubber. That is, the scrubber turns sulphur pollution into industrial sulphates.
