REFRACTION

We said in the earlier chapters of light, that light travels in a straight line. It does so, however, only if the transmitting medium has a uniform optical density (this is a property of transparent material that measures the speed of light through it).


For example, when a beam of light traveling in air is incident on the surface of water, part of the beam is reflected at the air–water interface or boundary. However, because water is transparent, most of the light is transmitted through it.


Since water has a higher optical density than air, the speed of light is reduced as it enters the water. This change in speed causes light ray entering water at an acute angle (angle less than 90o) to experience a change in direction abruptly. This change in speed and direction of light rays is known as refraction.


Therefore, refraction is a phenomenon caused by a change in the speed of light as it enters a transparent medium with a different optical density from the one it was formerly travelling in. The wave front representation and a picture on refraction are shown below.



Laws of refraction

  1. The incident ray, the reflected ray and the normal to the refracting surface at the point of incident are all in the same plane.

  2. The second law is known as Snell’s law and it states that, the ratio of the sine of the angle of incidence to the sine of the angle of refraction is a constant for a given pair of media. This constant is called the refractive index and it is denoted by n or µ.

One of the first tests made in a food testing laboratory to verify if butter has been adulterated with margarine was to measure the refractive index. Refractometer is the instrument used to measure refractive indices of substances.

Critical angle and total internal reflection

It is an established fact that, a ray of light which passes from a medium of higher optical density into one of lower optical density bends from the normal. What happens as the angle of incidence is increased gradually?


In ‘a’ of the picture below, the angle of incidence is small and greater portion of the incident ray enters the air.



If the incidence angle is increased gradually, the refracted angle also increases until a limiting angle is reached where the refracted angle is 90o as shown in ‘b’.




At this point, the refracted ray does not enter the air but rather takes a path along the air–water interface. This limiting angle is called the critical angle.


The critical angle of a medium is the angle of incidence in an optically dense medium, resulting in an angle of refraction of 90o. If the incidence angle is increased beyond the critical angle, the rays are totally reflected back into the water as shown in ‘c’.




In such a situation we say total internal reflection has occurred. Total internal reflection is a phenomenon where light travelling from an optically denser medium to a less dense medium is totally reflected back into the denser medium. The picture below is a practical experience of total internal reflection.



The two conditions that must be satisfied for total internal reflection to occur are:

  1. Light must travel from an optically denser medium to a less dense medium.

  2. The angle of incidence should be greater than the critical angle.


Mirage: this is an optical illusion in which part of the sky or a tall object appears to be reflected onto the ground in a hot day. In hot sunny days, the ground gets hot and thus, heats up the few layers of air closer to the ground to different extents.



This heated air becomes less dense than the few cold layers above. When a ray of light travels from the sky or the tip of a tree towards the ground, it will go through the different layers of air with different densities, the ray is refracted gradually as it goes through the different layer until a point where a viewer from afar sees the image of the sky or the tree as being reflected from a pool of water some distance ahead on the ground.


The conditions necessary for the formation of a mirage are:

  1. Weather must be sunny

  2. Temperature of atmosphere should be high

  3. Smooth surface or tard road

  4. Presence of air layers


Dispersion of Light

Suppose a narrow beam of white light falls on a glass prism in a dark room, the light which leaves the prism is separated into bands of different colours with each gradually blending into the other as shown below. The order of the colours is red, orange, yellow, green, blue, indigo and violet (taking the initials of the colours in turns: ROYGBIV).



It is evident that white light is a polychromatic light. Light consisting of several colours is known as polychromatic light; light consisting of only one colour is known as monochromatic light. Dispersion of light is the separation of a polychromatic light into its constituent colours. It is evident from the picture above that the angle of refraction for red light is not as great as violet light, and that the angles of refraction for the other colours fall between these two. Thus the refractive index of substances is not the same for light of different colours.


Formation of Rainbow

Rainbows are formed based on refraction and total internal reflection. When a ray of sunlight falls on a water droplet in the atmosphere, the ray is first refracted and as a result dispersed.


The refracted monochromes are transmitted through the water. At this point, the rays will be travelling from water to air, and since water is denser than air the rays are total internally reflected at the water–air boundary. The rays are refracted further as they pass out of the water finally and the visibility of the colours is more enhanced.


COLOURS

Colour is a property of light waves that reach the eye and detected by it. For instance, all colours of light are part of the electromagnetic spectrum including infra red (IR) and ultra violet (UV) rays.


IR and UV are not detected by the eye because their wavelengths fall outside the visible range of the eye, and hence they are invisible. The colour of light is dependent on the wavelength of the radiation coming from the source.


On the other hand, objects absorb certain wavelengths of the light incident upon them, and reflect others. Therefore, colour is not a property of objects but rather of the light.


For example, consider a piece of cloth which looks blue in sunlight. When this cloth is held under red light in a dark room it appears black. The colour of opaque objects depends upon the kind of light they are capable of reflecting into the eye.


Red, green and blue are monochromatic lights and are termed as primary colours. Primary colours cannot be produced by mixing other colours of light. The mixing of primary colours produces polychromatic light which is also termed as secondary colours.


Secondary colours are obtained by mixing any of the primary colours. Also, when any two colours of light combine to produce white light we say the colours are complementary colours. The pictures below show the primary colours and their corresponding secondary colours that can be produced by mixing them.


Note that white light is produced from mixing all the primary colours.

LENSES

Lenses can be made from almost any transparent substance that is bounded by two nonparallel, curved surfaces or by one plane surface and one curved surface. There are two types of lenses; they are the convex or converging and the concave or diverging lenses.



The only difference between the terms associated with curved mirrors and lenses is the optical centre. This is the centre of the lens and it falls on the principal axis.


Below are the various types of lenses.



Properties of image formed by a Concave Lens

  1. Virtual.

  2. Upright.

  3. Diminished.

  4. Formed in front of lens but before object.


Properties of images formed by a Convex Lens

Case 1; when object is placed between f and lens

  1. Virtual.

  2. Upright.

  3. Enlarged.

  4. Formed in front of lens but behind object.



Case 2; when object is placed at f

  1. Image is formed at infinity



Case 3; when object is between f and 2f

  1. Real

  2. Inverted

  3. Enlarged

  4. Formed beyond 2f at the other side of the lens


Case 4; when object is placed at 2f

  1. Real

  2. Same size as object

  3. Inverted

  4. Formed at 2f on the other side of the lens


Case 5; when object is placed beyond 2f

  1. Real

  2. Diminished

  3. Inverted

  4. Formed between f and 2f behind the lens
