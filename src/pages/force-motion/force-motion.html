FORCE AND MOTION

Newton’s Laws of Motion

We discussed Force and Motion in the last two chapters. In this chapter we shall consider the relationship between force and motion. Sir Isaac Newton (1642–1772) formulated three physical laws that tell us why objects move, by describing the relationship between force and motion. These laws are known as Newton’s laws of motion.


Newton’s first law of motion (law of inertia): States that a particle will stay at rest or continue at a constant velocity unless acted upon by an external unbalanced force. The first point seems relatively obvious to most people, but the second may require some thinking, because we have no experience in every–day life of things that keep moving forever (except celestial bodies).


If one slides a book along a table, it doesn't move forever, it slows and eventually comes to a stop. This is so, because frictional force acts on the body and in a direction opposite the motion. It is this force which causes the object to slow to a stop.


The first law also explains the phenomenon of inertia indirectly. Inertia is the tendency of a particle to resist a change in its state of motion. For instance, if you are standing in a crowded bus and the driver makes a sudden stop, your body continues to move forward because of inertia.

Newton’s third law of motion (law of interactions) states that to every action there is an equal and opposite reaction.


From the picture below, the air travels downward and the balloon in the opposite direction.



Newton made it clear from his third law that if two bodies come into contact or collide, they exert equal but opposite forces on each other. This means that the sum of the momentums of the colliding bodies before and after the collision is equal.


Since the sum of momentum before and after the collision is equal we say their momentum is conserved and t his led to another principle in physics known as the law of conservation of linear momentum.


The principle is properly stated as; the sum of linear momentums of colliding bodies before and after collision are equal if no external force is applied.


Mathematically, conservation of momentum is expressed as: m1 u1 + m2 u2 = m1 v1 + m2 v2

Note that this equation is a general formula, because the equation might change slightly depending on the direction of the velocities of the colliding bodies.

their common velocities if they stick together after collision.


Solution

If the direction of the fist body is considered positive, then that of the second will be negative. The momentums before the collision will then be written as:

m1 u1 ― m2 u2

Since they stack together after the collision, they will then have a common velocity, v. Hence their common momentum is given as:

(m1 + m2)v

Equating the two expressions:

m1 u1 ― m2 u2 = (m1 + m2)v

2 x 3 ― 4 x 2 = (2 + 4)v

Since the velocity is negative, it implies they travel in the direction of the second body.
