ACIDS

It has long been noticed that aqueous (water) solutions of some solid compounds conducts electricity. Ordinary table salt solution is a typical example. A solution that conducts electricity is called an electrolyte.



Further searches proved that there are three categories of compounds whose aqueous solutions are electrolytes. These compounds are acids, bases and salts.

WHAT IS AN ACID?

In 1887, the Swedish Chemist Svante Arrhenius defined an acid as any substance which forms hydrogen ions when dissolved in water OR an acid is a substance that contains hydrogen, which can be replaced partially or wholly by a metal. This hydrogen is called replaceable hydrogen. For instance, HNO3 is said to be an acid because it dissociates (splits) in water to produce H+ ions which combine with H3O to form H3O+ (Acid). The reaction equation is written below.


HNO3 + H2O H3O + NO3-(aq)


Arrhenius definition was limited in some areas, this lead Bronsted and Lowry to come out with their definition which is broader and better than Arrhenius’. They defined acid as a proton donor. For example, Arrhenius definition fails when it is applied to the reaction between ammonia and water because no H3O+(aq) are formed at the end of the reaction. The equation for the reaction is written below.


NH3 + H2O NH4+ + OH-


After an acid had donated a proton, the particle left is known as the conjugate base whilst the particle which is formed after the base has accepted the proton is known as the conjugate acid. For the reaction above, the acid and its conjugate base are H2O and OH- respectively.


Note:

  1. Arrhenius acid is specifically H+ or H3O+ and nothing else.


  2. H+ is a proton. Reason; hydrogen has an electron and a proton. Therefore loosing the electron leaves only a proton which is the H+ ion.


In 1923, the same year Bronsted and Lowry proposed their acid base theory, an American chemist Gilbert Newton Lewis, proposed even a broader definition of acids. He defined an acid as an electron pair acceptor.


Both Arrhenius and Bronsted-Lowry theories have limitations. For example, they can not be used to identify the acid or base in the reaction between ammonia and boron trifluoride (BF3), no H+ ion is produced neither is a proton donated in that reaction. However, an electron pair was donated by ammonia and boron trifluoride accepted the electrons.


Acids can then be defined based on the three theories above.


  1. Arrhenius Theory; an acid is a substance which forms hydrogen ions when dissolved in water OR an acid is a substance that contains hydrogen, which can be replaced partially or wholly by a metal. This hydrogen is called replaceable hydrogen.


  2. Bronsted-Lowry Theory; an acid is a proton donor. It provides protons or H+ ions.


  3. Lewis Theory; an acid is electron pair acceptor.


Properties of Acids

They;


  1. have sour taste


  2. have pH less than 7


  3. are very corrosive


  4. change blue litmus paper to red


  5. react with bases to form salt and water


  6. react with substances containing Trioxocarbonate (IV) ion (CO32-) to give effervescence of CO2.


  7. react with fairly reactive metals to form salts and evolve hydrogen gas.


Types of acids

Acids that are made from mineral substances are known as inorganic acids or mineral acids. Examples are hydrochloric acid, Tetraoxosulphate (IV) acid, Trioxonitrate (V) acid and many more. Acids that occur naturally are known as organic acids. Examples are citric acid, lactic acid, palmitic acid, ascorbic acid, etc.


Strong and weak acids

Strong acids: these are acids that dissociate (split) or ionize completely in water. Most mineral acids are strong acids.

Weak acids: these are acids that ionize partially in water. All organic acids and some mineral acids are weak acids. Example of a weak mineral acid is Trioxocarbonate (IV) acid (H2CO3).

Basicity of an acid

This refers to the number of hydrogen atoms that can be produced or released per a molecule of an acid. For example, ethanoic acid (CH3COOH) contains four hydrogen atoms, but only one hydrogen atom can be released from a molecule of the acid when it is dissolved in water, therefore its Basicity is 1.


Acid Basicity

H2SO4 2
H2CO3 2
HNO3 1
H3PO4 3

Preparation of H2SO4 by the Contact Process


Sulphur is burnt in the presence of oxygen to form sulphur (IV) oxide.


S + O2 SO2(g)


The sulphur (IV) oxide (SO2) is mixed with oxygen and heated over Vanadium (V) oxide (V2O5) to form sulphur (VI) oxide (SO3). The reaction follows the equation below:


2SO2 (g) + O2(g) 2SO3(g)


The hot sulphur (VI) oxide is dissolved in concentrated H2SO4 to form oleum (H2S2O7):


H2SO4 (l) + SO3(g) H2S2O7(l)


Note that directly dissolving SO3 in water is impractical due to the highly exothermic nature of the reaction. Acidic vapour or mists are formed instead of liquid acid.


Oleum is reacted with water to form concentrated H2SO4.


H2S2O7 (l) + H2O (l) 2 H2SO4 (l)


Uses of some acids

H2SO4: it is used in the manufacture of fertilizers, explosives, dyestuffs, other acids, parchment paper, detergents and glue. It is also used for purification of petroleum and pickling of metal.


HCl: it is used in the manufacture of chlorine containing-compounds.


HNO3: it is used to manufacture fertilizers.


H2CO3: it is used in the manufacture of fizzy drinks, for example, coca cola.


Ethanoic acid (CH3COOH): manufacture of vinegar, flavouring in food, etc.
