ELECTRICITY

The slight crackling sound that is heard when a dry hair is brushed and the tendencies of thin sheets of paper to resist separation are examples of the phenomenon of electrification. Electrification is the process of producing an electric charge on an object. Charged objects may attract small bits of cork, papers, or other lightweight materials.


Since the electric charge is confined to the object, it is said to be electrostatic charge. Electrostatics is electric charge at rest or static electricity. The law of electrostatics states that like charges repel each other and unlike charges attracts each other.


Static electricity is usually produced by friction between two surfaces in intimate contact. On the other hand, if an electric charge is in motion current electricity is produced. A solid material that allows the flow of electric current through it is known as a conductor.


A substance whose aqueous solution conducts electric current is known as electrolyte. Examples of Electrolytes are salts, acids and bases. Materials that do not allow the flow of electricity through them are known as insulators. Examples of insulators are glass, plastics, etc. Our discursion will be limited to the second type of electricity, current electricity.


Current Electricity

Electric current is generated from different forms of energy. The common sources are the cells (solar and electrochemical) and generators.


Electrochemical Cell (simply called cell); this is a device that transforms chemical energy into electrical energy. Electrochemical cells produce electricity through chemical reactions. During the reaction, electric current is produced as a result of the movement of charged bodies (electrons and ions) within the cell. There are two types of Electrochemical cells. They are:

  1. Primary cells; not rechargeable. Examples are watch batteries, Sunwat battery, etc.

  2. Secondary cells; are rechargeable. Examples are car battery, cell phone batteries, etc.


Generators

This is a device that converts mechanical energy into electrical energy. It operates on the fact that when a conductor is moved across a magnetic field, electric potential is induced.


This induced potential causes electric current (current for short) to flow in the conductor, only if it is a part of a closed circuit.


Note that current flows in closed circuits only. The major components of the generator are a field magnetic to produce the required magnetic field; and a conductor (armature) to conduct the generated current to an external circuit.


A shaft is connected to the armature to give it the required movement in the magnetic field by rotation. Hydroelectric, nuclear reactors, wind and thermal electric plants employ the use of generators produce electrical energy.



Under hydroelectric power generation, water is allowed to fall from a height (dam) onto a turbine. A shaft connects the turbine to an armature which is placed in a magnetic field. The weight of the falling water turns the turbine. The armature rotates as a result of that to generate electricity.


Nuclear reactors and thermal plants provide heat to boil water. Steam under high pressure is produced from the boiling water. This is used to turn a turbine which is connected to an armature through a shaft, just like the hydroelectric.


Wind power generation is much like the hydro. Here, wind is used to turn the turbine instead of water. The processes thereafter are the same as the hydro power generation.


Solar cells

This is a device that converts radiant energy from the sun into electrical energy. It is made up of a glass tube with a vacuum created in it. The surface of the glass tube is coated with a photosensitive material.


When light rays from the sun falls on the photosensitive material, electrons in the material absorbs certain amount of energy from the sun rays. They then jump off from the surface of the material unto the collector. This movement of electrons closes the circuit. And hence, current flows through the circuit.


Electric current

When two bodies, one positively charged and another negatively charged are joined with a conductor (copper wire), electric current will flow from the positive body to the negative. The electric current constitutes a flow of charged bodies in a conductor.


Electric current is then defined as the rate of flow of electrical charge pass a given point in a conductor. The SI unit of electric charge is coulombs. If the time rate for the charge(s) to flow is measured in seconds, then, electric current is given as C/s, coulombs per second. The SI unit of electric current is the Ampere (A).

TRANSFORMER

This is a device for changing an alternating voltage from a lower potential to a higher potential or vice–versa.



In principle, the transformer consists of two coils; the primary and the secondary coils which are electrically insulated from each other and wound on the same iron core.


Electric energy is transferred from the primary coil to the secondary by means of the magnetic flux which is set up in the core iron when an alternating current is applied to the primary coil.

Household Electricity Supply

In areas where electricity from a power station is available, it is brought to the house by means of thick electrical cables called mains electricity. The electricity is distributed around the house to sockets and electrical appliances are connected to the supply of electricity by pushing plugs into the sockets.


The supply is 240 volts alternating current in most countries. Because the mains electricity is alternating, none of its terminal can be viewed as positive or negative. Therefore, the terminals are viewed as live and neutral.


The neutral wire is connected to earth at the power station and it is at a zero (0) voltage. The live wire is at 240 volts and it should never be touched under any circumstance.

If a person touches it accidentally, the voltage from the power supply will push a current through him to earth. The current will be larger if his clothes or body is damp and the effect can be fatal. This effect is called an electric shock.

Fuse

When electric current flows in a conductor it generates heat. The heat generated depends on the quantity of current flowing in the conductor. Beyond certain current loads, the heat generated melts the conductor.


This principle is used in fuse to protect electrical gadgets from being damage by high electric current. The fuse consists of a thin wire which is made up of metal alloy. When large current that can damage appliances is passed through it, it melts and thus the circuit gets broken. This prevents the large current from getting to the appliance and damaging it.



Earthing

Sometimes the live wire can touch the metal frame (outer case) for an appliance such as an electric kettle. This will make the metal be at a potential of 240 volts. Anyone touching the metal will receive a shock.


To avoid this, the metal frame is connected to earth by an extra wire. This extra wire is connected to the metal frame of the appliance and through the plugs and to the earth underneath the house. The connection from the plug to earth may be through a metallic rod driven into the ground underneath the house.


If the metal part of the appliance is accidentally touches the live, a large current is driven to earth and melts the fuse. This cuts off the supply and no one receives a shock.
