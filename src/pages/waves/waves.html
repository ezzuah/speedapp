WAVES

If a stone is dropped into a quiet pool of water, the spot where the stone enters the water is disturbed. The disturbance, however, is not limited to one spot only. The particles disturbed by the entrance of the stone disturb particles adjacent to them, and these, in turn disturb other particles adjacent to them and so on. This sets up a wave which travels through out the water to other parts. A wave is a disturbance which travels through a medium and transfers energy from one point to another without causing any permanent displacement of the medium. As the disturbance moves through the water, it does not move individual particles very far from their equilibrium positions. After the disturbance passes, the particles return to their equilibrium positions.


Classes of Waves

  1. Electromagnetic waves; theses are waves that do no require a material medium for their propagation. Examples are light rays, radio waves, x – rays and gamma rays

  2. Mechanical waves; these are waves that require a material medium for their propagation. Examples are sound waves, water waves and wave generated in a string.


Waves can also be classified into two types based on the direction of their motion. These are:

  1. Transverse waves; these are waves in which the direction of travel of the wave is perpendicular to the direction of vibration of the individual particles of the medium. Example are water wave, wave generated by plucking a string such as guitar.


  2. Longitudinal waves; these are waves in which the direction of travel of the wave is parallel to the direction of vibration of the individual particles of the medium. Examples are sound waves, wave generated when a compressed spring is released.


Progressive waves are waves in which the wave profile moves along the speed of the wave. Both the transverse and longitudinal waves are examples of progressive waves.


Stationary waves are waves that are obtained when two progressive waves of equal amplitude and frequency are travelling in opposite directions and combine.

The Wave Equation

The speed of any wave can be calculated from the wave formula


V = f x λ

Where V = speed of wave,

f = frequency and

λ = wavelength.
