MAGNETISM


We know from certain elementary school activities that magnets do not attract materials like wood, stone, plastics, etc. These materials are called non–magnetic materials. On the contrary, magnets attract certain metals like iron, steel, and many more.


This second class of materials is called magnetic materials. This information is enough for a layman and a pupil but not a senior high school student. We shall increase our knowledge about magnets at this level.


Physicist noticed that magnet attracts some substances strongly. These substances are called ferromagnetic substances. Example of a ferromagnetic material is iron, steel, nickel, cobalt and some alloys like alnico and permalloy.


There is another group of substances that are repelled feebly by a magnet and they are referred to as diamagnetic substance. The third is a group of substances that are feebly attracted by a magnet and they are referred to as paramagnetic substance.


If a ferromagnetic material is brought closer to a magnetic gradually, it gets to a point where the magnet begins to exert an attractive force on the material. This region is within the magnetic field of the magnetic.


Magnetic field is a region around a magnet where magnetic force is exerted. The line along which a magnetic force acts is the magnetic line of force. Magnetic lines of forces move from the North Pole to the South Pole.

Demagnetisation


This is the opposite of magnetization. It is the process through which a magnet losses its magnetism.


  1. The magnet is placed in a solenoid connected to an ac source. The set up is done such that the solenoid has its axis facing east and west. After a few seconds, the magnet is removed from the solenoid and taken far away.


  2. By heating the magnet until it is red hot and then placed with it poles facing eats and west while it is cooling.


  3. Dropping or hammering it repeatedly; or arranging them disorderly during storage will weaken the magnetism in the magnet.


Temporal and permanent magnetism


If a ferromagnetic, material like an iron rod, is in contact with a bar magnet, the material behaves like a magnet by attracting pieces of magnetic materials. If the magnet is taken away, the material looses its magnetism.


This kind of induced magnetism is referred to as temporal magnetism. Temporal magnets are easy to make. Likewise, they loose their magnetism easily. Materials that can be magnetised temporarily are called soft magnets.


Example is iron. Materials that can not be magnetised temporarily are called hard magnets. Hard magnets are difficult to magnetise but ones magnetised, it is hard for them to loose their magnetism. Example of a hard magnet is steel.


The earth’s magnetic field


The earth behaves as if it has a small bar magnetic inclined at a small angle to its axis as shown in the figure on the right. A suspended bar magnet align its self with the earth‘s magnetic. One of the pole points to the northern hemisphere and it is the North Seeking Pole or simply the North Pole.



The other pole points to the southern hemisphere and it is called the South Seeking Pole or simply the South Pole. This magnetic property of the earth is used by sailors, surveyors and pilots to find their way around using a compass.


Uses of magnets


Magnets are used in loudspeakers, compasses, magnetic lift, telephone receivers, power generators, etc.


Electromagnetism


Michael Faraday discovered that when a metallic conductor is moved across a magnetic field, an emf is induced in the conductor. Similarly, when a metallic conductor is carrying electric current, a magnetic field is built around the conductor.



Because of this interdependence of the electric and magnetic fields, it makes sense to consider them as a single coherent entity – electromagnetism. Nonetheless, electromagnetism usually refers to the magnetic effect produced around a conductor as a result of the current flowing through it.


The right hand grip rule provides information about the directions of the flowing current and the magnetic field around a conductor carrying a current. It states that, imagine one grips a conductor, like pulling on a rope, such that the thumb points in the direction of the current flow, then the other fingers will point in the direction of the magnetic field around the conductor.


Examples of devices that operate on the principle of electromagnetic induction are the power generators, induction motors and power transformers.



A transformer that increases the voltage of an a.c. source is called a step–up transformer. Step–up transformers have greater number of turns in the secondary coil than in the primary coil. Transformers that reduce voltage are called step–down transformer. Step–down’s have greater number of turns in the primary coil than in the secondary coil. Refer from transformer diagrams above.


Electromagnets are used in generators, induction motors and transformers. They also have similar uses as magnets.
