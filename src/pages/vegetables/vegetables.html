VEGETABLES

The definition of the word vegetable is traditional rather than scientific. Therefore the usage of the word vegetable is somewhat arbitrary and subjective, as determined by individual cultural customs.


Generally speaking, an herbaceous plant or plant part which is regularly eaten as unsweetened or salted food by humans is considered to be a vegetable. Mushrooms, though belonging to the biological kingdom Fungi, are also generally considered to be vegetables.


Nuts, seeds, grains, herbs, spices and culinary fruits are usually not considered to be vegetables, even though all of them are edible parts of plants. In general, vegetables are regarded by cooks as being suitable for salted dishes, rather than sweet dishes. Vegetables might be eaten raw or cooked.


TOMATO PRODUCTION

The tomato (Solanum lycopersicum) is an herbaceous, usually sprawling plant, similar to its close cousins, chili pepper and eggplant. It is a perennial, but often grown as an annual.


It grows, reaching to 1 to 3m in height, with a weak, woody stem that often vines over other plants. The tomato is grown worldwide for its edible fruits, with thousands of cultivars (varieties) having varying fruit types, and optimum growth in differing growing conditions.


The most widely grown commercial tomatoes tend to be in the 5–6 cm diameter range. Most cultivars produce red fruit; but a number of cultivars with yellow, orange, pink, purple, green, or white fruit are also available. Multicolored and striped fruit can also be quite striking.


Environmental Requirement

Tomato does well in a well drained sandy loam soil rich in organic matter content. They require an annual rainfall of 7000 to 1200 mm distributed evenly. The optimum temperature range is between 14oC to 30oC.


Land Preparation and Planting

The land must be cleared first. Tomato may be planted on ridges or flat beds. Planting distance is ranges from 80 x 80 to 100 x 100 depending on variety planted. The number of seeds per hole is 1 to 2. Planting is done to meet the beginning of the rains.


Post Planting Operations

Weed must be controlled to avoid competition for nutrients plants and weeds. Since tomatoes are sprawling plants, stakes must be provided to give them upright posture and prevent fruits from touching the ground. Fertilizer may be applied twice, that is, before planting and at flowering stage.


Diseases and Control

Tobacco mosaic virus: It can be a serious disease of tomatoes. There are several strains of the virus, some of which are generally known as tomato mosaic.



Severely affected plants are stunted, yields may be reduced by as much as 25 per cent and affected fruit may be unmarketable. Leaves of affected plants turn light and dark green or yellowish–green.


Green and yellowish–red mottling or rings appear on the fruit with sunken dark brown spots [Picture]. Internal browning of fruit can occur as well. Tobacco mosaic is well noted for being highly infectious. For example, it has been shown to persist in tomato root debris at a depth of 120 cm for at least two years.


The virus is easily spread when infected and healthy plants are handled together. This virus can easily be carried into the crop on clothing, tools and stakes. Tobacco mosaic is difficult to control. If a range of control measures is carried out effectively, the incidence of mosaic in crops should be significantly reduced.


Altenaria


Tomato curly top: The disease is caused by virus―Beet Curly Top Virus. Leaves of infested plants are dwarfed, crinkled, rolled inward, and cupped upward.



Veins on the underside of leaves usually have a purple discoloration, may be roughened, and often produce swellings or spine–like outgrowths. Roots are stunted. Young infected seedlings mat die. This disease is transmitted (vectored) from infected to healthy plants by a small insect called the beet leafhopper.


There are no chemicals available for controlling the virus, but several cultural practices can help reduce or eliminate infections. Although varieties that are resistance to curly top are not known, farmers may benefit from trying to identify cultivars that are somewhat tolerant of the virus. Good sanitation practices, such as weed and insect control, are also essential in limiting the occurrence of the disease.


Pests of Tomato

Some common tomato pests are worms (cutworm and hornworm), aphids, whiteflies, flea beetles and mite.
