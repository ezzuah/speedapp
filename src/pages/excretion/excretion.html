EXCRETION


The body takes the essential materials it will need from the food and water we take in. After this, what happens to those that are not needed? They are considered waste and as such are collected and disposed off through a process called excretion. Excretionis the release and removal of harmful metabolic by–products (waste) from the body.


The kidneys are the primary excretory organs but other organs also function in excretion. Before the kidneys are considered, let us first learn about how some other organs also function in excretion. Your skinhas sweat glands in it.


These sweat glands excrete perspirationwhich is a solution of water, salt, and some urea. Although perspiration is a form of excretion, we perspire to cool our bodies and not necessarily to rid the body of waste.


The body cools when we sweat because heat is lost as perspiration evaporates and thereby keeping the body temperature within normal range. The liverexcretes bile pigments, which are derived from the breakdown of haemoglobin. Bile pigments are changed into bile which is stored in the gall bladder before it passes into the small intestine.


The liver also excretes cholestrol and excess fat–soluble vitamins. The lungsremove carbon dioxide from the body and this process of breathing out the CO 2is called expiration.


The kidneysremove nitrogenous wastes and unnecessary materials such as salts from the blood and maintain the proper water balance in the body. The picture below shows the female human urinary system.



Each kidney is shaped like a bean and there is one on each side of the body. The outer part of a kidney is called the cortex, the inside is the medullaand the central, hollow cavity is called the pelvis.


Each kidney is composed of a million tiny units called nephrons. Each nephron is made up of Bowman’s capsulewhich has the shape of a cup. The Bowman’s capsule also narrows into a long, coiled tubule. In the center of each Bowman’s capsule is a bulk of blood capillaries called glomerulus. Other capillaries of the glomerulus surround the tubule. The glomerulus, in effect,


brings blood into the kidney through renal arteries. The capillaries which surround the tubule merge to form small veins. These small veins merge to form the renal veins, which returns purified blood to the general circulation.



This is how the kidney–excretion process begins. As blood travels through the body, it picks up urea(a nitrogenoous waste produced in the liver).


Also excess salts and irons which build up in the body are also picked up. Blood passing through the renal arteries is channeled into smaller and smaller arteries of the kidneys which lead to each glomerulus.


Pressure forcesmost of the water and other contents of blood into the Bowman’s capsules. The continuous flow of blood into the kidneys forces the liquid from the Bowman’s capsules into the tubules which filter it( Ultrafiltration). Cells lining the tubule transport needed materials such as food molecules, hormones, and irons from the liquid passing through.


These materials then pass on from the tubule cells to the surrounding capillaries ( Selective reabsorption). Almost all the water is returned to the blood. The cleansed blood travels to the renal veins and back to the general circulation.


The urea, excess salts and sometimes other substances along with a small amount of water are not reabsorbed but remains in the tubules. The combination of these materials is what we call urine. Urine passes from the tubules into the collecting ductsin the medulla and then to the kidney pelvis.


Leading from the kidney pelvis of each kidney is a tube called ureter. The ureter transports to a hollow storage area called the urinary bladder. When the bladder becomes filled, urine is forced through the urethra.
