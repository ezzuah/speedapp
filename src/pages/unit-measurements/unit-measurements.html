UNITS AND MEASUREMENTS

Fundamental (Base) Units And Derived Units

In physics and engineering, measurement is the activity of comparing physical quantities of real–world objects (weight, height, length, etc) and events (time). Established standard objects and events are used as references and these references are called units.

The standard units of measurement in science are a part of a measuring system called the International System of units (SI). The letters are reversed in the symbol because they were taken from the French word Le Systèm International d’Unités.


Only SI units will be used through out the lessons although there are other units that can be used to measure base units. Example, length can be measured by foot, mass by pound and etc. There are two forms of units, they are fundamental or base units and derived units.


Fundamental units are basic units that do not depend on any other unit. The table below lists the physical quantities which have base units, their SI units and symbols.

Physical quantity SI Unit Symbol
Length Meter m
Mass Kilogram Kg
Time Second S
Electric current Ampere A
Temperature Kelvin K
Amount of substance Mole Mol
Luminous intensity Candela cd

In SI, prefixes can be added to the base units to obtain different units of a convenient size for measuring larger or smaller quantities. For example, a kilometer is one thousand meters while a millimeter is one thousandth of a meter.


A kilometer is convenient for measuring the distance between cities. Millimeter is useful when measuring the thickness of a board. The commonly used SI prefixes and their equivalents are listed in the table below.

Derived Units

Other measuring units can be obtained by combining some (not any) of the base units. For example, if a boy runs a distance of 100m in 50s, then his speed can be expressed as the time he took to cover the distance which is 100m per 50s (100m/50s).


This speed can be scaled down or expressed in the simplest form as the distance he covers in a second which is 2m per a second (2m/s).


Therefore, derived unit is a unit which is a combination of other units. The table below is a list of physical quantities whose units are derived (units) and their symbols.

Volume

This is the space occupied by an object. One of the instruments that can be use to determine the volume of an object in the lab is the measuring cylinder. The picture on the right shows how to determine the volume of an irregular object using the measuring cylinder. First a measuring cylinder is filled with water to a reasonable level and the water level is noted V1 (60ml).



A suitable length of thread is tied to the irregular solid and it is gently lowered into the water till it is submerged. The new level of the water V2 is noted (95ml). Now, subtract the initial volume from the final one to give the volume of the solid.


That is volume of solid = (V2 – V1) = (95ml – 60ml) = 35ml. Note that volumetric readings should be taken at the meniscus.
