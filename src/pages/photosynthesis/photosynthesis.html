PHOTOSYNTHESIS


Do plants have alimentary canal like humans? How do they get food? Do they have organs that will break down complex food molecules into small absorbable ones? Plants do not have alimentary canal, neither do they have organs to breakdown complex food molecules and for that reason they manufacture their own food.


The food produced has simple molecular structure which can be used directly in cellular or internal respiration. The picture below summarizes the processes by which plants manufacture their food. This process is known as photosynthesis.



Photosynthesis is defined as the process by which green plants use light energy to convert carbon dioxide and water into carbohydrates. Photosynthesis starts with the trapping of sunlight by the chloroplast in the leaf. Water that has been absorbed by the root into the plant goes up the stem and into the leaf.


Carbon dioxide enters the leaf, and into the chloroplast through small openings at the back of the leaf known as stomata. At this point, the light energy is used to break and rearrange the water and carbon dioxide molecules to form a simple sugar (carbohydrate) through a series of chemical reactions.


These reactions are controlled by chlorophyll and enzymes. A summarised chemical equation for photosynthesis is written below.


6CO2 + 6H2O C6H12O6 + 6O2


After the carbohydrates have been produced, it is transported from the leaf to various parts of the plant for respiration and to be stored as food in some cases. Some plants have special organs that store excess carbohydrates for future use.


For instance, cassava stores carbohydrates in the root, maize in the cob, melon in the fruit, etc. Part of the oxygen which is produced during photosynthesis is used for cellular respiration and the excess leaves the leaf through the stomata.


Photosynthesis is a very important process and it comes with the following advantages listed below.

  1. All forms of live depend directly or indirectly on photosynthesis. This statement is true because if you consider the goat which does not undergo photosynthesis, it depends (feeds) on green plants which undergo photosynthesis. And a carnivore like the lion feeds on the goat which also feeds on the green plant.

  2. It reduces the carbon concentration in the atmosphere, thereby reducing green house effect.

  3. It helps to maintain water, carbon dioxide and oxygen balance in nature. In other words, it helps to regulate carbon dioxide, oxygen and water quantities in the world.

  4. It produces oxygen which is needed for respiration.

    CO2 + H2O C6H1206 + O2 CO2 + H2O C6H12O6 + O2

From the table above, it is obvious that photosynthesis and respiration are reverse processes. The products of one process are the reactants of the other and vice versa.


We said earlier on that plants require water, carbon dioxide, light and chlorophyll for photosynthesis. Let us consider some few experiments to show if all the three named substances are basic requirements for photosynthesis. We shall consider the absence of carbon dioxide first, followed by the absence of light and finally chlorophyll.


Experiment to show that carbon dioxide is necessary for photosynthesis

Place a potted plant in a dark room for not less than 24 hours. This is to deplete all starch in the leaves of the plant. Enclose one leaf in polythene bag containing sodium hydroxide as shown in the picture on the right. The role of the sodium hydroxide is to absorb the carbon dioxide in the air within the polythene bag.


This ensures that no carbon dioxide is available for the leaf enclosed in the polythene bag. Expose the plant to light, either the sun or any strong electric bulb, for some hours. Test the enclosed leaf and any other for starch.


Observation: the enclosed leaf will remain brown indicating the absence of starch, which in turn means no photosynthesis took place due to the absence of carbon dioxide. The other leaf will show a blue–black colour with iodine indicating the presence of starch. This shows that photosynthesis does not take place in the absence of carbon dioxide.


Experiment to show that light is necessary for photosynthesis

Place a potted plant in a dark room for not less than 24 hours. This is to deplete all starch in the leaves of the plant. Enclose one leaf in black polythene bag as shown in the picture on the right. This is to prevent the leaf from receiving light. Expose the plant to light, either the sun or any strong electric bulb, for some hours. Test the enclosed leaf and any other for starch.


Observation: the enclosed leaf will remain brown indicating the absence of starch, which in turn means no photosynthesis took place due to the absence of carbon dioxide. The other leaf will show a blue–black colour with iodine indicating the presence of starch. Meaning photosynthesis does not take place in the absence of light.



Experiment to show that chlorophyll is necessary for photosynthesis

Make a sketch of a variegated leaf like the one shown in the picture on the right. The plant from which the leaf was plucked should be supplied with water, carbon dioxide and light before plucking the leaf. Note the areas of green and yellow colours well. Test the leaf for starch.


Observation: it will be observed that the blue–black colour indicating the presence of starch is formed in the areas where the original colour of the leaf was green. This shows that photosynthesis does not take place in the absence of chloroplast. Note that chlorophyll give green plants their green colour.
