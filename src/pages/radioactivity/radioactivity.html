RADIOACTIVITY

We learnt about isotopy in the first chapter, we saw that atoms of some elements may not have the same number of neutrons in their nucleus (isotopes). Not all isotopes are stable. What do we mean by a stable isotope? A stable isotope is one which will not decay (give off energy or disintegrate) spontaneously (naturally).




In the other hand, unstable isotope is one which will decay spontaneously in order to become stable. The process by which an unstable atomic nucleus looses energy to form a stable nucleus by emitting radiations in the form of particles or electromagnetic waves is known as Radioactivity. The SI unit of radioactivity is the Becquerel (symbol Bq) = number of disintegrations per second. There are two types of radioactivity.


These are natural and artificial radioactivity. Natural radioactivity is the spontaneous disintegration of an unstable nucleus. And artificial radioactivity is the disintegration of a stable nucleus after it has been bombarded with a sub–atomic particle (proton or neutron). The rate or speed at which a radioactive material decays is given by its half–life. The half–life of a radioactive element is the time taken for half the amount of the original element to decay. During radioactive decay, three different kinds of radiations are emitted. They are:


  1. Alpha particles; this is a particle which consist of two protons and two neutrons. They are similar to a helium atom which has lost its two electrons thereby attaining a +2 charge. It is denoted by α. It is deflected by both electrical and magnetic fields because it is charged. It has the least penetrating power among the three radiations. They travel some few millimeters in air and are stopped by a thin paper card.



  2. Beta particles; this is a high speed electrons. It is denoted by β. It has no mass and a single negative charge. It is also deflected by magnetic and electric field as a result of its charge. It has a high penetrating power. It can go through paper card but can be stopped by a thin aluminum foil.


  3. Gamma rays; this is a high electromagnetic radiation and it is denoted by γ. It has neither mass nor charge. It is not deflected by electric or magnetic fields. This goes through both paper card and aluminum foil. It is stopped by a thick lead plate.




Nuclear Reactions

Scientists noticed that radioactive decay releases great deal of energy. They induce this kind of decay through two main reactions, the fission and fusion reactions. Nuclear fission is the splitting of a large nucleus to form two smaller, more stable, ones.


The process involves bombarding a large nucleus with a slow moving neutron. The bombarded nucleus absorbs the neutron and forms a highly unstable nucleus which exists for a very short period. The unstable nucleus formed then disintegrates into two smaller atoms which are more stable the original atom. The two newly formed atoms are the daughter atoms. In most cases, a number of neutron particles are emitted from fission reaction. If there are other fissionable nuclei (like the original one) around, these emitted neutrons are then absorbed by the fissionable nuclei and disintegrate. The process goes on repeatedly and becomes self–sustaining. This kind of repeated reaction is termed as chain reaction.



Nuclear fusion is a nuclear reaction in which at least one heavier, more stable nucleus is produced from two lighter, less stable nuclei. Nuclear fusion is what makes our sun work. Every second, this process transforms astronomic quantities (600 million tonnes per second) of hydrogen atoms into helium, with the liberation of a phenomenal amount of energy. The object of nuclear fusion is to reproduce this process on Earth on our tiny human scale.


The main difficulty associated with nuclear fusion is that before recovering the energy, the hydrogen must be heated to 200 million degrees. That is exceedingly hot! So hot, in fact, no known material resists such temperatures. So, what can be done? Up to date, fusion energy has been produced in a very brief period (the current record is one second). But the energy released from fusion reaction is much higher than that of fission.



Since the energy released in nuclear reactions is so great and exposure to the fuel (radioactive element) is a potential threat to human health, the reactions are mostly performed in an enclosure, nuclear reactor. In the nuclear reactor, the chain reaction is controlled to avoid explosion. The reactor has a control rod usually made of boron because boron absorbs neutrons.


If the reaction is too fast, more rods are then inserted into the reactor to slow it. If it is too slow, the rods are taken out of the reactor to speed up the reaction. The heat generated from fission reaction is used to produce electricity. The picture on the next page shows the expulsion of water vapour from a cooling tower of a radioactive power plant. This should give you a fair idea about the amount of energy produced from fission reaction.



Nuclear Equation

If a reaction changes the number of protons in the nucleus of an atom and an atom with different atomic number is obtained then the reaction is referred to as transmutation (nuclear change). Nuclear Equation is an equation written to represent transmutation. To write a nuclear equation these two rules must be satisfied.


  1. Rule 1. Mass number is conserved in a nuclear change. In other words, the sum of the mass numbers before and after the reaction must be equal.

  2. Rule 2. Electric charge is conserved in a nuclear reaction. The total electric charge before and after the nuclear change should be equal.

The conventional representation of a nuclear particle is;

Z		 X

where A = Atomic mass of the isotope (mass of protons and neutrons present), Z = atomic number of isotope (which represents total charge on isotope) and X is the chemical symbol.

Uses of Radioactive Nuclides

Radioactive dating: One of the most interesting applications of radioactivity is its use in determining the ages of rocks and dating relics and past events.


Tracers: Radioactive elements are used as tracers. For instance, radioactive–containing fertilizer can be introduced into an area (ecosystem) to trace the uptake and movement of nutrients within the area.


Food Irradiation: food gets spoilt for many reasons. These reasons may be as a result of the presence of bacteria, other micro–organisms and internal enzymes. For that reasons, canned foods are sterilized with gamma radiation to kill all bacteria and micro–organism before canning.


Power generation: radioactive elements are used as fuel in nuclear reactors for the production of electrical power.


Medical diagnosis and treatment: it is used to diagnose and treat some diseases. For example, iodine is an element which collects in the thyroid glands. Because it does, radioactive iodine is used to diagnose and treat thyroid disorders.



Pest Control: It is used to control reproduction in pests (insects) such as locust by sterilizing the male species. After these males have been sterilized, any mating between them and a fertile female will not result in reproduction leading to the extinction of their species.


Harmful Effect of Radioactivity

All radiations (particles and electromagnetic waves) have an effect on living organisms. If the radiation has enough energy, it can penetrate living cells and disrupt their functions. This disruption result in skin burns destruction of cells which can even affect unborn babies, leukemia, bone cancer and all other forms of cancer.
