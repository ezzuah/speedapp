EQUILIBRIUM AND STABILITY

Is it possible to tie a string to the middle part of a wooden rod, lift it and still have the rod in its horizontal position? What is your reason if your answer is yes? In the picture below, the string is tied to a specific section of the rod such that the mass of the part labeled ‘a’ equals the mass of the ‘b’ part.



This section of the rod coincides with the centre of mass of the rod. The centre of mass of an object is a point within it where all its mass may be considered to be concentrated.


The centre of mass of a body does not necessarily lie in the exact centre or middle part of it. It lies at a point where the mass of the body on one half (side) equals the mass on the other. For instance, we can tell from simple experience that the centre of gravity of the rod below will not lie in the middle because the masses of the side ‘a’ and ‘b’ are not equal.



Centre of gravity, this is a point in a body where all of its weight appears to act. The centre of mass and gravity of an object are at the same point. However, the centre of gravity acts downward whereas centre of mass has no direction. The centre of gravity of regular object can be determined as shown in the figure below.

How to determine the centre of gravity of an irregular object


The centre of gravity of an irregular object like a cardboard can be determined using a plumb line. Three small holes are punched in three different points as shown in figure on the right. First we suspend the cardboard on a nail at point ‘A’.



A plumb line is tied to the nail. When the plumb line comes to rest, it will pass through the centre of gravity of the object. A line is drawn on the cardboard along the string of the plumb line. The process is repeated for points ‘B’ and ‘C’. The centre of gravity lies at the point where all the three lines meet.


Stability of objects


A body is said to be stable if a vertical line drawn through its centre of gravity falls through its base. It is not easy to topple a stable body or the force required to topple a stable body is greater than one required to topple an unstable body.


The position of the centre of gravity of a body determines how stable a body is.


‍ ‍ ‍


The figure above shows the same brick in two different positions. Both bricks are stable. However, the degree of their stability differs. The force required to raise the brick from lying to standing as shown in ’A’ is greater than the force required to change its position from standing to lying.


In simple words, the lower the centre of gravity of a body, the more stable that body is. Also, the stability of an object can be increased by increasing the length of the base and lowering the centre of gravity. A bus is loaded under the passenger seat and not on top of it, because that will lower the centre of gravity and make the bus more stable.


States of equilibrium


  1. Stable equilibrium; an object is said to be in stable equilibrium if, when a small force is applied to displace it slightly, the object returns to its original position.


  2. Unstable equilibrium; an object is said to be in an unstable equilibrium if, when a small force is applied to displace it slightly, the object moves away from its original position.


  3. Neutral equilibrium; an object is said to be in an unstable equilibrium if, when a small force is applied to displace it slightly, the objects moves but maintains its new position.
