MANAGEMENT PRACTICES IN POULTRY

Poultry keeping is a full time business and for that matter a lot of attention and care should be given to the birds from the time they are hatched to their maturity period. In this section we are going to talk about the processes the egg goes through before hatching into a day–old chick, through to the maturity of the hatched chick. Since the common bird reared under commercial scale in Ghana is fowl, it will be used in all illustrations in this course.


Sources of Day–Old Chicks

The day old–chicks are hatched from eggs. The process of supplying adequate temperature or warmth, humidity, ventilation and turning of fertile egg until it is hatched is known as incubation.

The two forms of incubation are the naturaland artificial incubation. Under natural incubation, eggs are given to the broody hen to sit on it and hatch. In artificial incubation,eggs are put in a special machine known as incubator.

The incubator supplies the necessary warmth, humidity, ventilation, turning and finally hatches the eggs. The natural method is not appropriate for commercial poultry farming. The source of day–old chicks for commercial poultry farming is the hatcheries. Some of the prominent hatcheries in Ghana are Akate Farms, Asamoah and Yamoah Farms, Afariwa Farms, etc.


Brooding Day–Old Chicks

From the ages of 1 day to 6 weeks in the life of the birds is known as the brooding stageand it is the most crucial stage in the life of any kind of poultry. At this stage, the birds are weak and susceptible to infections. Even cold weather can cause a lot of fatality at this stage.


Management Practices in the Brooder House

During the brooding period, the young birds are kept in a special house known as the brooder house. It is usually a permanent concrete building with very good ventilation. Clean feed and water are provided to the birds in little quantities but as often as possible.


This is to avoid wastage and contamination of feed and water. The feed is put on flat feed trays to allow easy access and the water in water fountains. The level of feed trays and water fountain should be low enough that birds can access with virtually no difficulty.


Apart from the feed and water, heat and light is essential to the life of young birds in the brooder house. The heat is to keep them warm when it is cold whereas the light will help them locate feed and water 24 – 7 to facilitate their growth.


Before the birds arrive, the room must be heated to 32 oC – 35 oC. The heating equipment may be an electrical heater, a gas heater or coal pot and the light source may be electric bulb, gas lamp or lantern (not convenient).


When the temperature is too low the birds crowd around the heating source and when it is too high they run away from the heat source. Under optimum temperature, they spread out evenly. The figure on the next page shows the behaviour of birds towards heat source.

After the brooding stage, the birds are transferred to their permanent pen. Between the ages of 7 to 18 weeks the layer bird is known as grower. At this point heat and light is taken away from the birds. Above 20 weeks, the birds are likely to lay and they are now called layers.

Diseases, Causal Organism, Symptoms, Control and Prevention


Disease and causal organism Symptoms Control and Prevention
Newcastle disease

(viral disease)

  • Neck of affected birds twist.

  • Loss of appetite.

  • Sneezing

  • Swollen face.

  • Paralysis.
  • Sick birds must be isolated (culled).

  • Observe routine vaccination.

  • Use footbath when entering pen.

  • Ensure good sanitation

(protozoan disease)

  • Pass out bloody feaces.

  • Swelling and inflation of intestines.

  • Feathers become rough.

  • Affected birds doze while standing

 Administer coccidiostat every week or 2 when birds are between 3 to 12 weeks.



Avian influenza, commonly known as Bird flurefers to "influenza caused by viruses adapted to birds". ‘ Bird flu’ is a phrase similar to "Swine flu", "Dog flu", "Horse flu", or "Human flu", in that it refers to an illness caused by any of the many different influenza viruses that have adapted to a specific host, like swine, horse or humans.


The Avian Flu claimed the lives of at least 200 humans in Indonesia, Laos, Vietnam, Romania, China, Turkey and Russia in the year 2007.


Epidemiologists (medical scientists who study the transmission and control of epidemic diseases) are afraid that the next time such a virus mutates, it could pass from human to human. If this form of transmission occurs, another pandemic could result.


Thus disease–control centers around the world are making avian flu their top priority. If symptoms of a disease are not clear, you can take about two of the affected birds to the veterinary for diagnosis and drug prescription or some control measures.


Pest of Poultry

Aside diseases, pest or parasites can cause low productivity or death in birds. These organisms suck blood from the birds and this can result in any of the following

  1. Anemia (loss of blood) in birds

  2. Low productivity in layers

  3. Weight loss

  4. Stunted growth

  5. Heavy infestation can results in death

  6. Endo–parasites (worms) can cause diarrhea or gastro enteritis.


The flea, miteand louselive on the outside of their host (bird) and thus, they are called ecto–parasites. The tapewormand roundwormlive inside the bird and they are called endo–parasites.


Routine Activities in Poultry Keeping

  1. The water troughs should be removed, washed and filled with fresh clean water each morning.

  2. Birds must be fed twice everyday.

  3. The litter must be stirred regularly to avoid accumulation of ammonia on top of it.

  4. Records must be kept on production, stock of birds, mortality (death), income and expenses each day.

  5. Sick and unproductive birds must be culled (removed) to a different pen.

  6. When birds start pecking or show any sign of cannibalism, they must be debeaked. Debeakingis the removal of the upper portion of the beak

  7. Wash pens, laying nests and feed troughs whenever birds are sold out finally.
