SALTS

Salt is a substance formed when the hydrogen in an acid is replaced by a cation. They consist of the negative ion of an acid and the positive ion of a base. They are neither corrosive nor caustic.


Preparation of salts

Salts can be prepared in three ways

  1. Neutralization reaction (reaction between an acid and a base).

  2. By direct combination. Mostly, when a metal or metal oxide reacts with a non–metal salts are formed. Example, when magnesium is burnt in chlorine gas, magnesium chloride is formed.

  3. Double decomposition reaction (that is, two soluble salts are used to obtain two new salts where one is insoluble.), example, the reaction between aqueous solutions of silver nitrate and sodium chloride (refer from page 3 of ‘Mixtures, compounds and Separation of Mixtures’).


AgNO3 + NaCl AgCl(insoluble) + NaNO3


Preparation of Zinc Sulphate (ZnSO4):

It is prepared from the direct combination of Zinc and sulfuric acid. The reaction equation is written below.


Zn + H2SO4 ZnSO4 + H2


Add excess zinc to dilute sulphuric acid in a beaker. The reacting mixture will give effervescence, when the ceases, filter the excess zinc and heat the solution (filtrate) to concentrate it. Allow the solution to cool after concentrating it for crystals to form. Filter the crystals off and recrystalise. Filter of any remaining liquid and dry the crystals on a filter paper. ZnSO4 can also be prepared from the reaction between zinc and copper sulphate solution.


Zinc sulphate is used to supply zinc in animal feed, fertilizers and agricultural spray.


Preparation of lead (II) nitrate [Pb(NO3)2]

Lead (II) nitrate does not occur naturally, therefore, it can be produced from Lead (II) nitrate which readily available as a mineral. Excess lead (II) oxide is dissolved in aqueous nitric acid. The reaction proceeds according to the equation below. Filter off the excess lead (II) oxide, concentrate the filtrate, allow the concentrate to cool for crystals to form and filter off the crystals.


PbO(s) + HNO3 Pb(NO3)2 + H2O


Uses: it is use for the production of lead paints (note that lead paints has been superseded by less toxic pigment such as titanium oxide).


Warning: Lead (II) nitrate is toxic, an oxidising agent and is categorised as probably carcinogenic (can cause cancer) to humans by the International Agency for Research on Cancer. Consequently, it must be handled and stored with the appropriate safety precautions to prevent inhalation, ingestion and skin contact. Due to its hazardous nature, the limited applications of lead (II) nitrate are under constant scrutiny.


Soluble and Insoluble Salts

Some salts are soluble whilst others are not. The table below gives a summary of soluble and no soluble salt.

Salt Solubility in water
Carbonates All are insoluble except sodium, potassium and ammonium carbonates.
Chlorides All are soluble except silver and lead chlorides
Sulphates All are soluble except barium and lead sulphates. Calcium sulphate is slightly soluble
Nitrates All are soluble

TYPES OF SALTS

Normal Salt

It is formed when all the replaceable hydrogen ion of an acid is replaced by a cation. The reaction between a strong base and a strong acid always produces normal salts. Examples of normal salts are NaCl, KNO3, CaSO4, etc.


Acidic Salt

This is a salt formed when only part of the replaceable hydrogen ion of an acid is replaced. Acidic salts are acidic in solutions. They produce H+ ions and behave like an acid. An example of an acidic salt is NaHCO3. NaHCO3 can be formed by blowing CO2 through concentrated NaOH solution. A whitish precipitate of NaHCO3 is formed.


NaOH(aq) + CO2(g) Na2CO3 + H2O(l)


Na2CO3 + H2O(l) + CO2(g) NaHCO3(s)


The reaction is a two step reaction. That is Na2CO3 will be formed first. Further addition of CO2 will then lead to the formation of NaHCO3


Basic Salt

This is formed when only part of the OH– ions of a base is replaced. Basic salts behave like a base in aqueous solutions. Example of basic salt is Mg(OH)Cl, Cu(OH)Cl.


Double Salt

They are salts which contain two different cations, example, KAl(SO4).
