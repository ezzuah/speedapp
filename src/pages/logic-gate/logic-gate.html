LOGIC GATE


Old–time radios used the vacuum tube―small light bulb–sized electronic tube with glowing filaments—to control the flow of electrons in a vacuum. The tube acted as a kind of switch. The last computer to use this kind of tube employed 18,000 of them and was weighing 30 tons (30,000Kg). The tubes developed faults very often and it took more than 15 minutes to identify and replace a faulty tube.


The transistor changed all that. The first transistor developed was one hundredth of a vacuum tube and was faster. In the early days, transistors were made individually and were later connected into electronic circuits with the use of wires and solders. Today, transistors are part of integrated circuits (IC’s)―an entire electronic circuit including wires all formed on a single chip, the microchip. A microchip is a tiny piece of silicon that contains millions of electronic circuit components which are mostly transistors. As IC technology advances, the required physical volume for each individual logic gate decreases and thus, digital devices of the same or smaller size are capable of performing ever–more–complicated operations at ever–increasing speeds.


Digital and Analogue Systems

The computer and other electronic devices may seem to be incredibly complicated devices, but their underlying principle is simple. When you open any electronic device (PC, calculator, radios, cell phones, etc.) what you see is mainly electronic circuits. And what is the most basic statement that can be made about electricity? Simply this: It can be either turned on or turned off.


In the two–state arrangements (on/off), the ‘on’ state may be represented as ‘1’ digit and the ‘off’ a ‘0’ digit. Note that the on/off mode does not necessarily mean turning the device on or off manually. But rather, current flows or does not flow through a specific circuit due to transistor arrangements. If current flows the transistor is turned on and a ‘1’ signal is sent and vice versa.


Since electronic devices operate on the on/off or 1/0 mode, they can represent any number using the two digits, 0 and 1. This system of representation is the binary system. Each off (0) or on (1) is called a bit—short form for binary digit.


In turn, bits can be grouped to represent numbers, alphabets, characters (punctuations) and many more. For example, the letter ‘A’ could correspond to the electronic signal ‘01000001’ (that is, off–on–off–off–off–off–off–on), the number ‘1’ to ‘00110001’ and ‘2’ to ‘00110010’.


A combination of eight bits is called a byte. Digital system or data specifically refers to an electronic signal or information which is in the binary state. Hence their values are either ‘0’ or ‘1’. Note that the binary system stated above is different from the binary bases (number base 2) in mathematics. If it was number bases, how then would alphabets, punctuation and characters be converted into base two?


Unlike digital data which is between 0 and 1, analogue data are continuous. For example, the values for temperature, pressure, velocity and many other physical quantities can fall within any range. Their data are forms of analogue data. Examples of analogue devices are speedometer, thermometer, pressure sensors and mass balance.


LOGIC GATES

Logic gates are elementary building blocks of a digital circuit. It performs a logical operation on one or more logic inputs and produces a single logic output. The transistor makes up logic gates.


There are several types of gates designed to respond differently to a particular patterns of electrical signals. Logic gates make up electronic circuits, (Note, however, that the gate is an electronic pathway and comprises of a set of electronic components that perform a particular task in an electronic system).


The circuits make up a logical device like the computer’s CPU. Most logic gates have two inputs and one output. In logic gates the ‘off’ state of the binary system is expressed as low voltage (which is approximately 0 volts) and the ‘on’ state as high voltage (approximately +5 volts).

The three gates discussed are the basic logic gates. Other gates can be derived from them by combining some (not any) of them. For example, the NOR (which is a short form for NOT OR), NAND (NOT AND) gate, XOR, etc. can be derived from the three.
