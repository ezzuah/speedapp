WATER

Water is a chemical compound made up of one oxygen and two hydrogen atoms. It has a chemical formula of H2O.


Physical Properties of Water

  1. Pure water is colourless, ordourless and has insipid taste.

  2. It has its maximum density of 1g/cm3 at 4oC.

  3. It is a weak electrolyte.

  4. It is neutral to litmus


  5. It freeze at 0oC and boils at 100oC at standard atmospheric pressure.


  6. It has a high surface tension.


Chemical Properties of Water

  1. It reacts with most metals to liberate hydrogen gas


2Na(s) + H2O (l) 2NaOH (aq) + H2


  1. Non–metallic oxides dissolve in water to form acids.


  2. Metallic oxides dissolve in water to form bases.


TYPES OF WATER

Water is classified as hard or soft depending on their easiness to lather with soap.


Hard Water


This is one that does not lather easily with soap. Hardness of water is caused by the presence of dissolved Ca2+ and Mg2+ and other polyvalent ions (A polyvalent ion is an ion that has more than a single charge; example is Al3+, Mg2+, SO42–, etc). Calcium usually enters the water as either calcium sulfate (CaSO4) or in the form of other mineral deposits. The predominant source of magnesium is dolomite (CaMg(CO3)2).When these ions are present in water and soap is added to the water, scum is formed instead of lather.


How Scum is formed

Soap is a salt formed from the reaction between a long chain fatty acids and a strong base like NaOH or KOH. Example is sodium stearate (CH3–(CH2)16–COO–Na). When soap is introduced into water it dissociates according to the equation below because it is soluble in water.

CH3–(CH2)16–COO–Na ‍ ‍ ‍water

.		 CH3–(CH2)16–COO – (aq) + Na+(aq)

After the dissociation, Ca2+ or Mg2+ present in the water will combine with the long chain anion to form an insoluble salt which is scum.

TYPES OF HARD WATER

Temporary Hardness

Temporary hardness is caused by a combination of calcium ions and bicarbonate ions in the water. It can be removed by the following methods.

* By boiling the water. Boiling promotes the formation of carbonate from the bicarbonate and precipitates calcium carbonate out of solution, leaving water that is softer upon cooling.

Ca(HCO3)2(aq) ‍ ‍ ‍boiling

.		 CaCO3(s) + H2O(l) + CO2(g)
* By the addition of lime (calcium hydroxide)

Ca(HCO3)2(aq) + Ca(OH)2 2CaCO3(s) + 2H2O(l)


Permanent Hardness
Permanent hardness is hardness (mineral content) that cannot be removed by boiling. It is usually caused by the presence of calcium and magnesium sulphates and/or chlorides in the water, which become more soluble as the temperature rises. Despite the name, permanent hardness can be removed through the following procedures.

By adding any soluble carbonate such as Na2CO3 to precipitate the calcium in the form of CaCO3.


Na2CO3 + CaSO4 CaCO3 + Na2SO4

  1. By distillation


Advantages and Disadvantages of Hard Water
Advantages Disadvantages
The calcium present help the formation of strong teeth Waste soap when it is used in the laundry
It has a pleasant taste Not suitable for dye and tanning industry
Prevents lead poisoning when lead pipes are used for water supply Produces scales in boilers and kettles


Note that when hard water is used for washing, the Ca2+ and Mg2+ present will form scum with the soap until no more of those ions are left to react with the soap before lather will begin to form. However, hard water has no effect on detergents because their salts with detergents are soluble and thus the water can still lather.


Soft Water

This is water that lathers easily with soap. It may or may not contain dissolved salts. Salts of Na+, K+ and NO3– may be present in water but they do not interfere with the action of soap. This means that soft water may or may not be free of dissolved salts but note that it is free from Ca2+ and Mg2+ ions.


Advantages and Disadvantages of Soft Water

Advantages Disadvantages
Saves soap when used for laundering Lack of calcium for strong teeth and bones
Suitable for the dyeing and tanning industry May cause lead poisoning when lead pipes are used for water supply
Does not form scales in boilers and kettles Has unpleasant taste


Treatment of Water

Water for municipal consumption is usually from rivers. The water from the river may contain leaves, dead organic material, silt and various kinds of impurities. It is therefore essential to be treated before consumption. At the water supply station, the water is first pumped through a coarse grid to remove large impurities. It is then moved to a settling tank and alum is added to settle all the suspending particles. The water is moved again through a sand filter to remove micro–organisms. Finally, chlorine is added to it to kill bacteria and other pathogens before it is pumped for supply.


Water Cycle

Water from the sea, rivers, lakes and ponds evaporates into the atmosphere. The evaporated water rises, cools and condenses in the atmosphere as clouds which are tiny water droplets. These droplets come together to form large bulk of water that falls as rain or snow. This process repeats itself and a cycle is formed known as the water cycle.
