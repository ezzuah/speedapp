SOUND

We all have experience of some sound involving activities; music from a loud speaker, a crying baby, teacher instructing students and many more. Sound is a form of a wave, which travels through an elastic medium from a vibrating body to a listener.


It is a longitudinal wave, meaning, the wave profile and the vibration are in the same direction. Sound is produced and transmitted by vibrating matter. It cannot be propagated in vacuum or in the absence of matter. It is therefore classified as mechanical wave, since it always requires a mechanical medium for its propagation.


The set up on the right can be used to prove the fact that material medium is required for the propagation of sound. In the set up, an electric bell is suspended inside an airtight jar. The jar is connected to a vacuum pump.



The electric bell is then operated and set ringing. As air is pumped out of the jar, it is observed that the sound of the bell gets fainter and fainter though the hammer is still striking the bell. It gets to a point where no sound is heard.


This is when all the air has been pumped out of the jar. The sound is heard again when air is pumped into the jar. This shows that sound cannot be propagated in a vacuum but requires a material. Its sources include bell, drum, loud speaker, vibrating fork, etc.


The Speed of Sound

Since sound is a wave, its speed may be calculated from the wave formula. In practice, the speed of sound varies greatly from medium to medium depending on the density and elasticity of the medium in which it is travelling.


Medium Speed
Air 330m/s
Water 1500m/s
Steel 5000m/s


From the above table, it is obvious that the speed of sound is larger in media with greater densities. Rise in air temperature increases the speed of sound. For every 1oC rise in air temperature matches a 0.6m/s rise in the speed of sound. Temperature change in liquids and solids does not affect the speed of sound appreciably.


Characteristics of musical note

  1. Pitch; it is a measure of how high or low a note (sound) is.

  2. Loudness; it is the sensation in the mind of an individual listener, depending on the intensity of the sound. In other words, it is the effect of the intensity of sound waves on the ear. Loudness, however, decreases as the listener moves away from the source.

  3. Quality; the quality of a note is the characteristics that distinguish it from other notes of the same pitch and loudness played on a different instrument. For example, the sound produced by different instruments in an orchestra can be differentiated from each other, even though the sounds played may have the same tone with equal intensity.


Reflection of Sound (Echoes)

Echo; this is a reflected sound from a hard surface. Echoes are undesirable in big halls because they cause interference. Recording studios and concert auditoriums cover their walls and ceiling s with soft perforated boards and not hard smooth materials.


This is to avoid reflection of sound waves or echoes, which may be heard several times and cause the room to reverberate (echo) for a longtime. The soft perforated board minimizes reflection by absorbing the sound waves quickly.


Echo has very useful applications such as determination of speed of sound; measurement of the depth of the sea in echo sounding devices as shown in the picture below, the exploration for crude oil and many more.



Refraction

Sound wave may also be refracted. Just like light, the speed of sound changes as it travels from one medium into another of different density from the former one.


Doppler Effect

You have probably heard the sound of a moving police siren. You will notice that the pitch of the siren dropped abruptly as the vehicle passed. The variation of the pitch heard from a moving source of sound is called doppler effect. This variation in pitch is as a result of changes in the vibrations that reach the ear per second (that is the frequency sound).



Uses of Doppler Effect

  1. It is used to determine the depth of the sea

  2. It is used to check the speed of moving vehicles by traffic policemen.

  3. Bats use echoes to notice obstacles in their path


X – RAY

This is an electromagnetic wave of high frequency, between 1018 to 1019 Hz. There are two types of x – ray. They are the soft and hard x – rays. The properties of x – ray is listed below.

  1. It is not deflected by magnetic or electric field. That is, it is neutral.

  2. It travels in a straight line.

  3. It may cause ionization in gases.

  4. It can penetrate most materials.

  5. It is diffracted by crystals.

  6. Causes fluorescence in some substances.

  7. Has a short wave length

  8. Travels through vacuum (applies to all electromagnetic waves like radio waves)


PRODUCTION OF X – RAY

They are produced by bombarding a metal target with high speed electrons. The high speed electrons are produced by accelerating it through a high potential difference. When the electrons hit the metal target, their velocity is brought to zero (0). They give off their kinetic energy in the form of x – ray.



USES OF X – RAY

  1. It is used to detect bone fracture in human beings.

  2. It is used to check cracks and leakages in a machine.

  3. Used for analyzing the crystal structure crystals.

  4. It is used to treat tumor.

  5. It is used to check hidden weapon in a baggage.

  6. Soft x – ray is used for the treatment of skin diseases

  7. Hard x – rays are used for the treatment of deep cancer growth.


HAZARDS OF X – RAY

  1. Causes skin burns.

  2. It deforms unborn baby when pregnant mother is exposed to it for long periods.

  3. Since x –ray can penetrate the body, it causes ionization (ions are produced) which can change or destroy living cells.
