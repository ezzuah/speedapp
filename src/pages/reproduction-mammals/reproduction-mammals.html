REPRODUCTION IN MAMMALS


‘How did I come into the world?’ Mostly we seem not to get answers to this and many other mind puzzling questions like this one. This topic of reproductionwill, at least, clear the air.


Humans are mammals and the study of human reproduction shows the basic pattern found in mammals in general. Below are the pictures of the (human) male and female reproductive systems as they are from the inside.



Female Reproductive System


On each side of a female’s abdominal cavity is one ovary(egg). The ovaries produce sex hormones. Very close, but not attached to each ovary, the oviductor fallopian tube. These two oviducts join into a thick– walled, muscular organ called the wombor uteruswhere implantationoccurs.


In effect, the womb houses the developing fetus(the unborn baby). The hollow womb narrows to a small opening called the cervixwhich leads to the vagina. The vaginais a canal which has an external opening. Note that the urinary and reproductive systems in the female are entirely separate.


For example, the urethra carries only urine, and the vagina receives penis during sexual intercourse, serves as birth canal and as an exit for menstrual flow.


External genitalsin females, including the vagina, like clitoris, labia(sing. labium) majoraand labia minoratogether add up to their sexual excitement during intercourse. The external genital organs of the female are known collectively as vulva.


The clitoris is homologous to the penis. Although quite small, the clitoris has sense receptors and allows it to function as a sexually sensitive organ which does erect when aroused.


Male Reproductive System


There are two oval structures within an external sac, scrotum, called the testes(sing. testis). In many mammals it is within the abdomen but in humans and some other mammals, the testes are external because the spermsproduced in them require a temperature slightly lower than body temperature.



So testes are suspended within the scrotal sacsof the scrotum. The sperm mature within the epididymis,a tightly coiled tubule lying just outside each testis. Without their maturation, sperms cannot swim to the egg.


The vas deferens(sperm duct, picture above) connects the epididymis to the urethra. Sperms are stored in both the epididymis and the vasa deferentia (plural). This means that in the male human, the urethra forms a passage for both urine and sperms. During ejaculation, sperms are forcefully ejected from the male’s urethra into the female’s vagina.


At this time sperms swim in a fluid called seminal fluid (semen). The pair of seminal vesicles, Cowper’sand prostateglands, add secretions to the semen. The penisis an erectile organ which is placed in the vagina during sexual intercourse (mating). Note that it is also through this same opening that males pass urine.


As noted above the female and male reproductive organs produce eggs and sperms respectively. These eggs and sperms are the sex cells. Also because the egg and sperm join together (fuse) during fertilization, they are known to biologists as gametes.


The female gamete (egg) is sphericalin shape like a dot (approx. 50 times the diameter of sperm) and contains some essential nutrients the developing embryo may need in the yolk(of the egg ).


Even in humans where most of the nutrients needed for the developing baby are taken from the mother, there is still some yolk content in the egg cell. The egg is produced once a month. The egg also carries one set of genetic informationin the nucleus.


The male gamete (sperm) on the other hand initially has two but loses one during the process of meiosis. So it also carries one set of genetic information.


Sperms need their long tail to swimso that they can get to the egg since the latter cannot move by itself. Their acrosomescontain enzymes which they use to dissolve any part of the egg membrane where they reach so as to penetrate.


Unlike the egg which is produced once every month, sperms are produced all the time and it is estimated that approximately 300 million sperm cells are ejected during each ejaculation. After both the egg and sperm are fully matured is when fertilization can take place.


During sexual intercourse(mating), semen is forcefully ejected from the male’s urethra into the female’s vagina. Although millions of sperms may enter the vagina, only a few thousand of them pass through the cervix into the uterus (womb).


Still fewer enter the oviduct where fertilization usually occurs and only onefertilizes the egg. After intercourse and fertilization, a couple may expect that developmenthas begun. Sometimes a singlefertilized egg divides into two completely, producing children who look very alike.


They are then called Identical twins. When this single egg fails to divide completely, the children look alike though but are not separate from each other. In this case, they are called Siamese twins. At other times the ovary releases two different ova at the same time. This time two different sperm cells fertilize each of them resulting in children who do not look alike called Non– Identicalor Fraternal twins.


In cases where the ovary releases about three, four or more eggs at a time which also get fertilized by different sperm cells then triplets, quadripletsor more may result.


However, when fertilization fails to occur, menstration takes place. Menstrationis the loss of blood and tissue from the uterus at the end of a uterine cycle.


Control of Reproduction


Species reproduce mainly because they want to get young ones of their kind so that they don’t become extinct. It is established that humans and dolphines are the onlyliving creatures who have sex for pleasure apart from the intention of procreating.


As a result, humans have devised diverse means of controlling the rate at which they reproduce. Abstainanceis the most effective birth control method without risk. Contraceptives, on the other hand, are medications and devices that reducethe chance of pregnancy.

Sexually Transmitted Diseases (STDs)


These are diseases that are mainly contracted through sexual intercourse. Some of these are Hepatitis B, Acquired Immunodeficiency Syndrome (AIDS), Genital herpes, Genital warts, Gonorrhea and Syphilis.


It is said that Hepatitis Bis more likely to be spread through sexual contact than the HIV. It can also spread by blood transfusion and contaminated needles. About half of persons infected with the disease have flu–like symptoms, including fatigue, fever, headache, nausea, vomiting, etc. This disease, also like HIV, can be passed on from mother to child through the placenta.


In the picture below, the bald–headed man has been infected with AIDS. The disease is caused by a group of viruses known as Human Immunodeficiency viruses (HIV) that affect specific blood cells.


The mode of transmission is the same as that of Hepatitis B. Both diseases can be prevented by avoiding sexual intercourse with infected persons, avoiding blood transfusion from the infected person, abhorring the use of an already used blade, razor or any sharp–edged device.


Genital herpesis a viral disease causing eruptionsand painful blisterson the genital. Genital wartsare also a viral diseases causing firm genital elevated blemishon the skin of the genital.


Gonorrheais transmitted sexually from the infected person but is caused by a bacterium called neisseria gonorrhoeae.Affected person goes through pain when passing urine and a thick whitishurethral discharge 3–5 days after contact with affected person.


Gonorrhea can spread to other parts of the body causing heart damage. Avoiding intercourse with infected persons can be preventive. New born infants receive eyedrops of tetracyclineor penicillinas protective against infection leading to blindness since they came through the canal of parent who may be at risk.


Syphilisis also caused by the bacterium treponema pallidum. It starts with ulcerated sorewith hard edges which usually heal leaving little scars. Then skin rashwhich does not itch spreads all over the body, even in palms and feet.
