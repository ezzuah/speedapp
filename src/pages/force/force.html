FORCE

The role of forces in everyday life is a familiar one. Pushes and pulls of all sorts. A horse pulls a wagon, a baby throws a rattle, a boy stretches a spring and a motor raises an elevator. These are common examples of force involving motion.


However, the nonexistence of motion does not mean absence of force. For example, a horse can exert a force on a block of heavy stone and yet will not move the stone. This means that force can cause motion, but it is not always correlated with motion.


What motion will be caused if a baby pushes on a wall? Forces can stop motion as well. For example, a footballer brakes a moving ball with his foot.


Forces can also cause deformation in some situations. For example, if you hit a milk tin with a hammer. Right now, it is obvious that force can cause motion, will try to cause motion, stop motion or deform a body.


Therefore, FORCE is defined as that which causes or tends to cause a change in a body’s state of motion or its shape. The SI unit of force is Newton (1N = 1kgms-2).

Mathematically, the force possessed by a body is given as,


F = ma; Where F = force, m = mass of the body and a = acceleration of the body.


Example 1

A boy rolls a ball of mass 0.1kg on a level ground for it to gain an acceleration of 8ms-2. Calculate he force applied.

Solution

F = ma

m = 0.1kg; a = 8ms-2.

F = 0.1 x 8

F = 0.8N


Weight: The weight of a body is defined as the amount of gravitational pull on the body. Weight is considered as force because it is expressed mathematically as W = mg and its unit is Newton. Where W = weight of the body, m = mass of the body and g = acceleration due to gravity. This equation can be compared with F = ma.


F = ma
W = mg


The bold alphabets in the two equations are the only differences.


Since g reduces as we move away from the centre of the earth, the weight of a body is not a constant value. The value of g on the surface of the earth is 9.81ms-2. The weight of a body taken on a high land (as in mountain top) will be lesser than one taken on a low land.


In practice, no single force exists. Any applied force (action) has an opposing force (reaction). In ‘A’ (the figure on the next page), a ball bounces back after hitting a wall. Note that the ball has mass and acceleration.


Meaning, it possesses some amount of force. It bounces back because the wall exerts an equal but opposite force on the ball. In ‘B’, similar action and reaction is experienced when a force F is applied to the block lying on the ground to cause a relative sliding motion.


Here, the force of friction that exists between the floor and the base of the block acts as the opposing force. If the force F applied is not greater than the frictional force, the block will not move.


Again, when you try to lift a bucket full of water, a downward force (weight of bucket + that of water) acts as the reaction to the force you apply which builds some sort of tension in your arm. Until the upward force you exert is greater than the weight of the bucket and water, it will remain in its position. Friction in liquids is termed viscosity.



Frictional Force in detail

This is the opposing force to relative sliding motion between two contacting surfaces.


There are two types of friction. They are


  1. Static friction: this is the force required just to overcome friction to cause a stationary body to begin to move.


  2. Kinematic or Dynamic friction: this is the force required to maintain (the motion of) a body in a uniform motion over the surface in which it is in contact with. Value of kinematic friction is less than static friction for a particular pair of surfaces in contact.


Laws of Friction

  1. The magnitude of the frictional force is dependent on the nature of the surfaces in contact. That is whether smooth or rough. Rough surfaces increase the magnitude of friction.


  2. For the same body in contact, the frictional force is independent on the area in contact.


  3. The frictional force acts in a direction opposite to the applied force.


  4. The magnitude of the frictional force is equal to the normal reaction between the two bodies multiplied by a proportionality constant known as the coefficient of friction (U).


The figure below is an illustration of the fourth law of friction. The normal reaction (R) is the perpendicular force the floor exerts on the block which is equal to the weight of the block. If no external force is applied to the block, then the only forces that will exist are the weight of the body and the reaction of the floor as shown in case 1.



Friction will come to play if and only if a force is applied to the block to move it as shown in case 2. The magnitude of the Frictional force


(Fr) is equal to U x R.

Since R = W

it can be rewritten as:

Fr = U x W.


The coefficient of friction (U) depends on the smoothness of the contacting surfaces. The smoother the surfaces in contact the lesser the value of the coefficient of friction and hence, the lesser the frictional force and vice–versa.


From the above illustration, if

F < or = Fr, then the block will be at rest, it will only be in motion if only

F > Fr.

Advantages of Friction

Friction between


  1. The feet and the ground make walking possible.


  2. The wheels of a vehicle and the road makes movement possible


  3. The foot of a ladder and the ground allows the ladder to lean on a wall without slipping.


  4. Enables the brakes of a vehicle to function properly.


There are so many examples that can be given. Try to figure some of them out.


Disadvantages of friction

  1. It generates unnecessary heat in machines


  2. It reduces the efficiency of machines since some force is required to overcome it.


  3. It causes wear and tear.


How to reduce friction

  1. By lubricating contacting surfaces.


  2. Smoothing rough surfaces which are in contact through polishing.


  3. By the use of bearings and rollers



Viscosity: This is the internal friction of a fluid. Viscosity of fluids decreases with increase in temperature and vice–versa. Viscous fluids as compared to non–viscous fluids do not have free flow. It is therefore expected that water should flow faster than condensed milk, because water is less viscous. Viscosity also affects the motion of bodies in a liquid.


For example, a piece of stone will fall faster to the bottom of a container containing water than one containing condensed milk.


Comparing the viscosity of two liquids: Put equal volumes of the two different liquids whose viscosities are to be compared in two measuring cylinders as shown in the picture below.



Gently drop a metal ball into each cylinder and note the time it takes each ball to get to the bottom of the cylinder. The liquid in which the ball gets to the bottom faster is the less viscous.


Uses of viscosity

  1. Aircraft and ships move because of viscosity in air and water respectively.


  2. Viscous fluids are used as lubricants. A more dense liquid serves as a good lubricant than a less dense one.


Types of Forces

  1. Centripetal force: this is a force which draws a body moving in a circular path towards the centre of rotation.


  2. Centrifugal force: this is a force which draws a body moving in a circular path away from the centre of rotation.


    The figure below demonstrates centripetal and centrifugal forces.

  3. Frictional force: this is the force that opposes relative sliding motion between two surfaces in contact. Fluid friction is called Viscosity or Viscous Drag.


  4. Tension: this is a force in a stretched string or spring.


  5. Gravitational force: this is the force of attraction between any two bodies placed in a gravitational field.


  6. Electrostatic force: this is the force attraction or repulsion between two charged bodies.


  7. Electromagnetic force: this is the magnetic force built as a result of the motion of a charged body.
