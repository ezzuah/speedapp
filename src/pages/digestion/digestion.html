DIGESTION IN MAMMALS

Sometimes we eat mainly because our bellies are light. When a diet contains adequate amounts of all the necessary nutrients required for healthy growth and activity then that diet is balanced.


Some of these nutrients are proteins, carbohydrates, fats, vitamins, mineral salts, etc. After eating, the food is digested before it can serve all those useful purposes. Digestion deals with how food is converted into absorbable substances by our mammalian body. Just take a careful look at the picture below.




The food we eat is taken in (ingested) through the mouth, or oral cavity. The major function of the mouth is to physically digest the food.


That is, our teeth grind and tear the food into chunks or balls small enough to be swallowed. Besides this, the food is being moistened by the saliva produced by the three pairs of salivary glands.


The saliva contains mucin which makes the food slippery so that it can be swallowed easily. Saliva also contains an enzyme called salivary amylase which begins the chemical digestion of food, especially starch.


From the mouth, the moistened, slippery and slightly digested food passes through the pharynx (throat) and into a long tube called the esophagus ( also oesophagus). As the esophagus transports the food, it also adds a chemical called mucus to it thereby facilitating the digestion process.


Food is moved at this point and at other parts of the digestive system by a series of muscle contractions and relaxations called peristalsis. The food next moves to the stomach. The stomach is a thick–walled, J–shaped organ that lies on the left side of the body beneath the diaphragm.


The stomach is a continuation of the esophagus above and the duodenum below. The stomach stores food and can expand to accommodate large amounts of it. In fact, protein digestion starts in the stomach.



Gastric glands form the lining in the stomach wall. Gastric glands secretes a fluid called gastric juice into the stomach.Gastric juice contains hydrochloric acid (HCl) and a protein called pepsinogen. The HCl gives gastric juice a pH between one and two.This pH level changes the pepsinogen into an enzyme called pepsin.


Pepsin then converts proteins into amino acids. By the time this phase of digestion is completed, food in the stomach is now an acidic liquid called chyme. The chyme then goes into the small intestine.


The first part of the small intestine is the duodenum where the food passes into the jejenum. From the jejenum, the food passes into the last and longest portion of the small intestine called the ileum.Note that the pancreas and gall bladder, though, are not part of the alimentary canal but are crutial to the digestion process.



The pancreas releases (secretes) several enzymes: Lipase which breaks fat molecules into fatty acids and glycerol. Pancreatic amylase changes starch to maltose (a white crystalline sugar formed during the digestion of starch) and trypsin which breaks down undigested proteins in the stomach.


The bile is a greenish substance produced by the liver. The gall bladder is an organ which stores bile. The bile contains bile salts which breaks up fats in the intestine. This process of fat breakdown is called fat emulsification. Note that bile is not an enzyme.


At this point, fingerlike projections on the intestinal lining of the small intestine called villiabsorbs glucose and amino acids into the circulatory system while the fats are absorbed into the lymphatic system (see Transport in Mammals).


This means that in the small intestine, all complex food substances are converted into simpler forms. Undigested material and water are passed from the small intestine to the large intestine, or colon.




The large intestine is divided into three sections the ascending, transverse and descending colons (refer from picture on page one).


No digestion takes place in the large intestine. The major function of the large intestine is to absorb the water from the undigestible or undigested materials. This causes the waste matter to become a solid. Solid waste called feces is stored in the rectum and is egested ( eliminated from the body) through the anus.
