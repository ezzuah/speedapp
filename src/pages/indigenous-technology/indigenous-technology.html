INDIGENOUS TECHNOLOGY


Most of the things our grandparents did were based on science. But then, they never bothered about investigating the results of their actions. We shall discuss the scientific principles underlying some of their operations.


Grinding stone/Pot and Blenders: Grinding stone (pots) were far in used, even now, before the introduction of blenders. They work on the same principle, friction. As the small grinding stone moves to and fro over the bigger one, with the grinding material in between them, friction is generated. The material wears bit by bit and that result in the grinding process. Most homes with modern kitchen nowadays use blenders. Blenders do not operate on friction. It has a sharp cutting edge at the base. A small induction motor is connected to the cutting edge. When it is plugged, the motor rotates the cutting edge which cuts the materials. The blender has an advantage of working fast and saves manual effort. However, it can not be used to grind materials like tiger nut which the stone can. So can you design a machine that will save time, manual effort and yet grind hard materials?


Earthenware coolers and Refrigerators: Earthenware cooler, simply known as cooler, is made from clay and has a pot–like shape. It is used to store and cool drinking water. The underlying principle is evaporation. The water kept in the cooler seeps through the tiny pores to the outer surface of the cooler. As the water on the outside evaporates, it absorbs latent heat of vapourisation (refer from Change of State, Heat) from the cooler thereby reducing its temperature. The water in the cooler gets colder as the process continues. In the absence of a cooler, cooler–like bowl made from a good conducting material (metal) can be used to cool water in a similar way. This can be done by wetting a cloth of reasonable size. Wrap the cloth around the bowl. As water evaporates from the cloth surface it cools the bowl, and the cooling effect is eventually transferred to the water. Refrigerators work in a slightly complex cyclic manner. A sub cooled refrigerant (cooling liquid) is pumped through cooling tubes situated inside the refrigerator in a cyclic manner. The refrigerant absorbs heat from the cooling compartment and thus cools the compartment.


Maize Drying: Up to date, some farmers in the rural areas dry their maize under the roof of their kitchen. This maize are left on the cobs and hanged to wait for the next planting season. Smoke from fired firewood dries, and protects the maize from weevil and other pests attack. This process is a pure chemical process. Smoke contains a chemical known as formaldehyde which acts as pesticides and the heat acts as a dryer. This process works perfectly, but the question is what quantity of maize can be preserved this way? Foreign technology has improved upon these two processes—drying and pesticide application. Commercial dryers that can dry about 80tons or 80,000kg of maize in an hour have been built. Chemicals that curb pest attack in the storage have been synthesized. Silos or warehouses for large storage of maize have also been built.


Lantern and Electric Bulb: Lanterns operate on capillary and combustion. The kerosene rises up the wick under capillary action (as in chromatography). Burning of the fuel to give light is the combustion process. Combustion releases CO2 into the atmosphere. The CO2 aids green house effect or global warming. Meaning this method of lighting is not convenient. Besides the green house effect, the light produced in not bright. Electric bulbs also work on resistance of conductors. When conductors carry electric current, the generate heat. This heat generated heats the conductor until it glows to produce light. In fluorescent lamps, a gas at low pressure is used to produce bright light. Light produced under modern technology is much brighter and economical.


Separation of Kernel from Shell in Palm Kernel Extraction>

Palm kernel extraction in villages is done by cracking the kernel with stone. The kernel must be separated from the shell before the actual extraction begins. Hand picking of the kernel from shell will slow their working rate so badly. So, extractors use a liquid separation process which involves flotation. This process begins with pouring the kernels and shells into a big pan filled with water. The materials (shell and kernel) sink to the bottom. Now, clay is added to the mixture with stirring gradually. As the amount clay added increases to a point, the shells float to the top. Can you explain why the shell is floating at this point? Reason: As the clay is being dissolved in the water, the density of the liquid (clay suspension) increases steadily until a point where the density of the suspension lies between that of the kernel and the shell. At this point, the kernels are denser than the liquid phase, which is also denser than the shells. Hence the shell floats and the kernel sink. The shell can be scooped or decanted from the liquid surface. What substance do you think would be hygienic and cheap but still serve the same purpose as the clay? Have you considered salt? Is there any other?


Why indigenous technology is not growing?

The building block of technology is thinking. Unfortunately, our education system does not give enough room for thinking. Why is this assertion a fact? For example, in Ghana, a specific definition for force may run through all science text books and students are required to produce exactly that or else be punished. If students are given keywords and understanding around which they could weave their definitions, studying would not have been a ‘chew–and–pour affair’. As a result, the student who is able to memorise a lot is considered brilliant.


This has gone a long way to affect our rate of innovation (when it comes to science, engineering, art, pharmacology, medicine, business, etc.). And the cause of this chew-and-poor attitude is that teachers prepare students for examinations and not the challenges of life problems so most engineering graduates are unemployed or employed in areas which are irrelevant to their course of study.


Secondly, research has never been a hallmark of the average Ghanaian from time immemorial till now. Simple observations or questioning of some behaviour could have brought up some sort of innovation. For example, termites do not feed on the leaves of the teak tree and why is it so? Does the leave contain chemicals that can be use in the production of anti-termite products?


Another problem is that Ghanaians do not believe in local technology. For example, the Chemistry Department of the K.N.S.U.T. manufactured an indelible ink for elections, but the government by–passed this product and imported a similar one from India. Fuller earth (activated clay) used to remove red pigment in palm oil to produce vegetable oil is imported in large quantities by Unilever Ghana Ltd. The company has long over–looked similar local technology developed by the Chemical Engineering Department, K.N.U.S.T. in producing fuller earth from clay at Pankrono, a suburb of Kumasi.
