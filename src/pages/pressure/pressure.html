PRESSURE

When you are hit with the edge of a ruler, it hurts more than the flat side. This is so because, the surface area of the edge is smaller, therefore it will exert a greater pressure than the flat side although the same magnitude of force may be applied.


Similarly, a knife with a thin (sharp) cutting edge requires a little force to cut an object. Pressure is defined as the force applied to a unit area perpendicularly. When the surfaces in contact are small, you need a little force to apply a large pressure. The SI unit of pressure is Pascal (1 Pa = 1 N/m2)


Mathematically, pressure, P, is expressed as

P = F
A

Where P = pressure; F = applied force and A = area


Pressure in Liquids

What happens when your friend sits on your lap? Your thigh feels the pressure his/her weight exerts. In a similar manner, if a body is submerged or buried from the surface of a liquid, the (volume of) liquid above the body exerts a pressure that corresponds to its weight.


The magnitude of pressure on the body depends on the density of the liquid and how deep the body is buried below the liquid surface. Denser liquids exert greater pressure, and on the part of depth, the deeper the body from the liquid surface the greater the exerted pressure.


Let us find out how to calculate the pressure at any point (depth) within a liquid. Consider a circular disk with a cross sectional area ‘A’ at a height ‘h’ below the surface of a liquid with density ‘’. The pressure that the liquid exerts on the disk can be calculated as follows.



The volume of the liquid above the disk is given as;

v = A x h

Making mass the subject in the density equation,

Implies that mass = Density x volume

Mass = ρ x A x h

And therefore the weight (mass x acceleration due to gravity);

W = ρ x A x h x g

Where g = acceleration due to gravity

The pressure on the disk, P;

The theory above can be substantiated by filling a barrel or any suitable container with water as shown in the picture on the right. Three holes with equal diameters are punched vertically on the barrel to make the water flow out.



The flows of water from the barrel through the various holes will be at different rates due to pressure differences at the various heights. The force with which water flows through the hole closest to the base is proof that water at that level is under greatest pressure.


This is so because of the pressure, the weight of the above water (molecules) exert. Looking at the middle hole, the water (molecules) above that level is not as much as the base hole.


Therefore it experiences a pressure lower than the base hole and thus has a lower flow. The same rule of thought can be applied to the top hole. This illustration confirms the fact that pressure in liquids increases with depth.


Characteristics of Pressure in a liquid

  1. The pressure in a liquid increases with depth.


  2. The pressure at any point in the liquid acts equally at all directions


  3. The pressure at all points at the same level in a liquid are equal.


Atmospheric Pressure

The atmosphere around us is made up of air, dust paricles and water moisture. All these substances have weight. Hence, they exert pressure on the surface of the earth as well as all objects on its surface (even you).


The magnitude of this pressure measured at sea level is about 100,000 Pa. We know that pressure in a liquid increases with depth, consequently, we should expect that atmospheric pressure will also increase or decrease with height. The atmospheric pressure in a valley would be greater than that on a mountain top.


PUMPS

A pump is a device that moves fluids (liquids or gases) and fluidized solids (example, a mixture of rice husk and air) from one point to another. Examples of pumps are, lift pump, force pump, vacuum pump, bicycle pump, centrifugal pumps and many more.


The Lift Pump

When the piston moves downward as shown in figure below, A, the pressure in the pump increases, valve 1 closes and 2 opens. Water then flows through 2 to a collection point.


When the piston moves up as in B, the pressure in the pump reduces and water then flows from the source into the pump.



The Force Pump

When the piston moves up as shown in figure , A, the pressure in the pumping chamber reduces, valve 2 closes and valve 1 opens. This causes water to flow from the source into the pumping chamber.


When it moves downward as in B, the pressure in the pumping chamber increases, valve 1 closes and 2 opens. Water then flows through 2 to a collection point.



Note that for both the lift and force pumps, if the pressure in the pumping chamber falls below the atmospheric pressure, water then flows into the chamber until the pressure within the chamber equals the atmospheric pressure. Generally, all flow processes are as a result of differences in some physical property. For example, electric current flow as a result of differences in electric potential, heat as a result of differences in temperature and fluids as a result of pressure difference.


Car Braking System

The figure below shows a schematic diagram of a car braking system. When the driver applies a force to the brake pedal, the force is transmitted through the fluid. As the pedal pushes the brake fluid in the master cylinder, the fluid pressure in both the master and slave cylinders are increased equally.




This increase in pressure will cause the slave cylinder to push the brake shoe outward. When the brake shoe touches the wheel, the car stops. The return spring brings the brake shoe back to its original position when the applied force is taken off.
