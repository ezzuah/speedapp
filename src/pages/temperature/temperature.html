TEMPERATURE

We know from experience that heat and temperature are related. However, they are not the same. The temperature of a burning match is much higher than an electric heater (reason: when a paper comes into contact with a burning match, it will burn faster than when in contact with a plugged electric heater). However, the burning match cannot warm a bucket full of water but the heater can.


Therefore, heat can be seen as the amount of thermal energy a body contains, accepts or evolves. And temperature is a measure of the degree of hotness or coldness of a substance. When two bodies of different temperatures are in contact, heat flows from the hotter body to the colder body.


Temperature Scales

There are many temperature scales (units) but we will narrow our discussion to only two; which are the degree Celsius (oC) and degree Kelvin (K).

  1. Degree Celsius/Centigrade; this is a temperature scale which has the freezing point and boiling points of water as its reference point. The ice point or lower fixed point (freezing point of water) is fixed at zero degrees Celsius (0oC).

    And the steam point or upper fixed point (boiling point of water) is fixed at hundred degrees Celsius (100oC). The degrees (temperatures) between these two fixed points are divided into hundred equal parts. From one degree to the next is a temperature interval of one degree Celsius, 1oC.

  2. Kelvin; this is a temperature scale that has its reference point at the absolute zero (–273K) of all substances. At that temperature all the potential and kinetic energy contained in a substance have been removed. The temperature interval between the Kelvin and the Celsius are the same.

    For example, the freezing point of water is zero degrees Celsius, 0oC which is equal to 273K. And the boiling point is hundred degrees Celsius which is equal to 373K. Note that the temperature difference between the freezing and boiling points of water in both the degree Celsius and the Kelvin is 100 temperature units.


Thermal Expansion

With few exceptions, substances (solid, liquid or gases) expand when heated and contracts when cooled. When the temperature of substance is raised, its thermal energy increases. The increase in thermal energy increases the amplitude of vibrations of the atoms or molecules of the substance.


As a result, the atoms and molecules move to equilibrium positions slightly further apart than before, and the solid expands in all directions. When equal volumes of gas, liquid and solid are heated, the gas expands more than the other two and the liquid also expands than the solid.


Anomalous Expansion Of Water

Most substances expand as they are being heated. Water is an exception of this general trend of heating and expansion. When water at a temperature below 0oC is heated, it expands steadily until it gets to 0oC. It begins to contract between the temperature ranges of 0oC to 4oC and expands steadily again at temperatures greater than 4oC. This means that water has it greatest density at 4oC.


Change Of State

Matter exists in three physical states, which are solid, liquid and gas. One matter can change its normal physical state to the other depending on the physical conditions it is subjected to.


Melting and Freezing

When sugar is heated to a certain temperature it melts or fuse. The temperature at which a substance melts is the melting point. At the melting temperature, any heat applied to the sugar goes to break the bonds holding its molecules in fixed positions causing it to melt (flow). It is after all the sugar particles have been melted, when its temperature will begin to rise again. When the melted sugar is cooled (heat is removed from it), its temperature will be reducing until it gets to the melting point.


At the melting point, the temperature remains constant and the sugar solidifies. The melting point is the same as the freezing point. The freezing point is the temperature at which a substance changes state from liquid to solid. This means that melting and freezing of a particular substance occur at the same temperature (note that solids melt at that temperature whilst liquids solidify at that same temperature). Another example is supplying heat to ice at 0oC will melt it whereas removing heat from water at 0oC will solidify it. Impurities lower the freezing point of liquids.


Boiling and Condensation

Using a similar rule of thought, we can come to the conclusion that boiling and condensation occur at the same temperature. For example, liquid water boils at 100oC when it is heated whilst at that same temperature, water vapour will condense when heat is removed from it.


The following factors affect boiling point diversely

  1. The presence of impurities increases the boiling point.

  2. Reduced atmospheric pressure reduces the boiling point.

  3. Less dense liquids have a lower boiling point than denser liquids.


Evaporation

When some amount of robbing alcohol is placed in a shallow dish at normal room temperature, you will notice that the level of the alcohol decreases in a short time and the odor of alcohol become strong in the air around. We say the alcohol has evaporated. Evaporation is the process through which liquid molecules absorbs heat from its surrounding and escape to the gaseous state.


Evaporation causes cooling effect. For example, when you dip your finger in a robbing alcohol and allow air to blow over it, the finger feels cool. The alcohol absorbs heat from the hand as it is evaporating; this makes your hand feel cold. The human body employs evaporation to cool down. If you sweat on a hot sunny day, the sweat takes some heat from your body as it evaporates and your body cools down.


The rate of evaporation is increased by

  1. Increasing the surface area of the liquid exposed to air.

  2. Lowering the pressure on the liquid.

  3. Increasing temperature.

  4. Lower relative humidity of the surroundings.

  5. Increased in wind velocity.


Sublimation

Just as robbing alcohol, a piece of camphor left in the open air becomes smaller and smaller until it disappears eventually. The molecules of the camphor escape from the solid surface and go into the gaseous state. Sublimation is the process through which a substance changes state from the solid state to the gaseous state.

Clinical Thermometer

This is an example of a liquid in glass thermometer. It is used purposely to measure the body temperature of humans which is around 37oC for a normal human being. Therefore, the temperature range of the clinical thermometer is between 35 oC and 43oC.


The clinical thermometer has some special design features that make it function perfectly.

  1. It consists of a fine bore in which the mercury level rise or fall depending on the temperature it is exposed to. The fine bore in the thermometer ensures that the rise of mercury in the thermometer tube is uniform.

Properties Of A Thermometric Liquid

It must:

  1. expand uniformly with temperature.

  2. be visible.

  3. not wet glass (that is, must have low adhesive force).

  4. be a good conductor of heat.

  5. have freezing and boiling points outside the temperature range we wish to measure.


Why Water Is Not Used In Thermometer

  1. It expands irregularly at certain temperature range (between 0oC and 4oC).

  2. It vapourises and condenses at certain temperatures.

  3. It wets and clings to the glass tube.

  4. The temperature range between its freezing and boiling points is very small.
